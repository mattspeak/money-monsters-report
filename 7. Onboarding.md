# 7. Onboarding

## 7.1 What the Onboarding Experience Involved

The research stimulus included a short quiz-based onboarding sequence designed to assess participants' financial knowledge and attitudes before directing them into personalised lesson content. 

The quiz comprised of five behavioural and scenario-based questions about money situations and spending habits, guided by Mr. Money — a human character who framed it as a way to understand the participant's starting point, not test their competence.

## 7.2 What the Research Reveals About Onboarding Design

### 7.2.1 Behavior-based questions

Across all segments, respondents appreciated questions that asked how participants actually behave with money, not what they know.  Althea (19) put it directly: *"knowing a definition doesn't mean you have relevant experience or real-world need for it."* All participants across three segments understood that the quiz was assessing their situation, not testing their competence.

### 7.2.2 Length

Younger Teens found the research stimuli quiz length appropriate; Older Teens and Young Adults consistently wanted more, with several expressing that more questions would have allowed a more accurate picture of their situation and more tailored content.

### 7.2.3 Universal design requirements

**1. Frame the quiz as calibration, not assessment.** The framing — that the app is learning about the user, not testing them — should be explicit from the first screen. Make the purpose clear upfront: a brief statement ("we'll use your answers to choose content that's relevant to where you are right now") increases motivation to engage honestly, particularly among older participants sceptical of generic educational tools.

**2. Ask behavioural and situational questions, not knowledge tests.** Questions should ask how participants actually behave with money, not what they know. Scenario-based questions consistently outperformed definitional tests across all segments.

**3. Use age-appropriate amounts, terminology, and scenarios.** Issues arose where questions referenced financial products unfamiliar to younger participants (ISAs, cryptocurrency) or used amounts unrealistic for their income level. A question about a £500 windfall lands differently for a 13-year-old than a 23-year-old. Questions should be drawn from segment-calibrated pools, or scaled based on early signals.

## 7.3 Onboarding design by segment

**Young Teens:** Keep the quiz short (5–7 questions). Focus on money personality and near-term goals. Avoid financial product terminology; use amounts and scenarios from their actual financial world. 

**Older Teens:** A slightly longer quiz (7–10 questions) is appropriate. Include questions that acknowledge real income and impending transitions (further education, bank/savings accounts) to surface genuinely relevant content. 

**Young Adults:** Prioritise life-stage and behavioural questions. Future iterations of the app could get a sense of where the user is in their life and tailor content to support this. E.g. about to move out of home,  at or heading to university, starting a pension.
