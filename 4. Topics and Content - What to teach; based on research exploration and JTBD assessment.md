# 4. Topics and Content: What to teach?

The research did not ask participants to list a curriculum, though a picture of their financial lives emerged clearly: the decisions they face, the concepts that confuse them, the moments of stress, and the outcomes they want.

Topics and lessons below are drawn from three inputs: what participants explicitly said they wanted to learn; what their behaviours and anxieties implied they needed; and the Jobs to Be Done that define each segment's relationship with money. 

This is a prioritized map of what is most likely to be relevant, engaging, and consequential for each group at their current life stage — not an exhaustive curriculum.

---

## 4.1 Content Relevance by Segment

### Young Teens (12–15)

Young Teens' topics point toward foundation-level content — grounded in their current, small-scale financial world.

| Topic                                    | Serves Which Job To Be Done       | Evidence from Research                                                                                    | Priority           |
| ---------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------ |
| Saving basics and goal-setting           | Make money last; Save for goals   | "I can't save money. I just spend it." (Olivia); console goal (Sam, 2 years); football boots (Isaac)      | **High**           |
| Needs vs. wants                          | Make money last longer            | £11 at a shop "without thinking" (Zain); clothes impulse buys (Olivia, Lottie)                            | **High**           |
| What are bank accounts?                  | Head start; understand money talk | Uses Rooster (Norah); piggy bank vs. bank account (Isaac); "I check mine daily" vs. "I can't access mine" | **High**           |
| Resisting impulse spending               | Make money last longer            | Universal pattern: all 7 YT participants describe spending money the moment they receive it               | **High**           |
| Understanding adults talking about money | Understand money nobody explains  | Mortgages (Isaac, after parents' house move); taxes (Norah); investing (Hugo, after dad's conversations)  | **Medium–High**    |
| What is interest?                        | Head start; understand money talk | Conceptual intro; foundation for OT savings content                                                       | **Medium**         |
| What is investing?                       | Head start on peers               | Hugo's dad mentions it; genuine curiosity without expectation of action                                   | **Medium**         |
| Scam awareness                           | Don't get caught out              | Family scam stories (Olivia); parental warnings across 5 YT participants                                  | **Medium**         |
| Earning money (chores, side income)      | Head start; make money last       | Vinted seller Hugo; chores across multiple participants                                                   | **Not appropriate** |

> **NB:** Whilst 'earning money' emerged as a desired topic for Young Teens — several participants sell items, do chores, or expressed curiosity about generating income — we question whether a financial literacy product should actively encourage children under 15 to seek earned income. 
> 
> Where earning does arise in lessons, the responsible framing is around managing what you already have — not promoting earning as a solution.

**Suggested lessons to hook the interest of Young Teens**

- *Why does my money keep disappearing?* — The science of impulse spending; why it happens; simple tricks to slow it down
- *Save for the thing you actually want* — Goal-setting mechanics; splitting money; what happens to it over time
- *What's the difference between saving and spending?* — Needs vs. wants; mental frameworks for real choices
- *What even is a bank account?* — Account types explained simply; why savings pots matter; how banks hold money
- *What does interest mean?* — First introduction; savings interest as a reward for waiting
- *Adults keep saying "invest" — what does that mean?* — Demystifying the concept; no action required at this age
- *How to spot a scam* — Scams are getting harder to spot, can you spot these real examples?
- *Make your pocket money go further* — Practical mini-budgeting; a week in the life

**What to exclude at YT level:** Abstract long-term planning, mortgages and pensions as taught content (fine as vocabulary), complex investment mechanics, tax filing. These can appear as "words you'll hear" but not as lessons with actions attached.

### Older Teens (15–18)

Older Teens are approaching or already in their first financially independent situations; content should bridge their current reality and the transition ahead.

| Topic                                                 | Serves Which Job                          | Evidence from Research                                                                                                  | Priority        |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------- |
| Savings account types and how they work               | Make smarter decisions; Don't fall behind | "I know you can put money into savings... but what type?" (Ovie); Aiden using Revolut savings but uncertain about rates | **High**        |
| Scam recognition — how fraud works in practice        | Avoid scams and traps                     | £4,000+ YouTube scam content (Aiden); Telegram job scam (Enoch); pattern recognition vs. checklist                      | **High**        |
| Smart spending frameworks                             | Make smarter decisions                    | 50/30/20 rule resonated strongly with Aiden and Levi; "spending on what you actually need"                              | **High**        |
| Interest rates — how they affect you                  | Don't fall behind                         | Knowledge gap across all OT participants; foundational for savings and credit decisions ahead                           | **High**        |
| Credit scores — what they are and why they matter now | Don't fall behind                         | Approaching first credit products; "I want to feel ready, not out of depth"                                             | **Medium–High** |
| Investing 101 — should you start now?                 | Don't fall behind                         | "My parents invest — should I?" (Aiden, Ovie); real curiosity but no action readiness                                   | **Medium–High** |
| Cashback — what it actually means                     | Make smarter decisions                    | "I see it on my Revolut but I'm not sure what it means" — both Aiden and Ovie, independently                            | **Medium**      |
| First job and payslips                                | Don't fall behind                         | Levi (£12/hour, zero-hours contract); approaching first employment for others                                           | **Medium**      |
| Buy now pay later — what it really costs              | Avoid traps                               | Approaching first exposure to credit products; prevention framing                                                       | **Medium**      |
| Automating savings                                    | Make smarter decisions                    | Aiden's Revolut auto-transfer already working; scalable lesson                                                          | **Medium**      |

**Suggested lessons to hook the interest of Older Teens:**

- *Which savings account is actually right for me?* — Account types; why it matters; what interest rate means in practice
- *How scams work — and how they get people like you* — Pattern recognition; real tactics; how to respond not panic
- *The 50/30/20 rule — does it work for you?* — Practical spending framework; how to adapt it to part-time income
- *How does my credit score get built?* — Early credit building; what damages it; why starting now matters
- *Should a 16-year-old invest? (Honest answer)* — Honest framing; what to consider; how to start if the answer is yes
- *Your first payslip: what all those deductions actually mean* — Tax, NI, pension contributions; real-number examples
- *Buy now pay later: the small print you need to read* — How promotional rates work; what happens when they expire
- *How interest actually grows (for and against you)* — Interest on savings vs. interest on debt; same mechanic, different direction

**What to exclude at OT level:** Complex investment products; retirement planning; content that assumes either a full adult salary or pocket-money-only income. The financial baseline for this group is part-time earnings (£10–£500/month); all scenarios should reflect that range.

### Young Adults (18–25)

Financial education is most immediately consequential and require specificity, depth (with relevance) and actionable simulation

| Topic | Serves Which Job | Evidence from Research | Priority |
|-------|-----------------|----------------------|----------|
| Compound interest — the actual mechanics | Understand product mechanics | Grace learned it from the app demo and reacted with genuine surprise; Althea saving inefficiently without knowing it | **High** |
| Savings accounts and ISAs — what's actually the right choice | Understand product mechanics | "I don't really know how to use them... just putting them in another account which isn't the most efficient" (Althea); Grace's ISA knowledge gap | **High** |
| Credit cards — how they actually work | Don't get caught out | Mohammad paid interest on a balance he could have cleared because autopay defaulted to minimum | **High** |
| Credit scores — building and protecting them | Not feel out of depth | Mohammad's credit card mistake; Zain actively researching; approaching first major credit decisions | **High** |
| Budgeting for irregular income | Not feel out of depth | Mia (tutoring + part-time, 2 income streams); Althea (same pattern); Grace (student finance 3x year + bar work + parents) | **High** |
| Buy now pay later and promotional rate traps | Avoid traps | Friend's 0% promotional card (Mohammad); "the small print you don't read" | **High** |
| Investing — practical first steps | Understand product mechanics | "Friends are getting into it" (Mia, Althea); Zain researching ISAs and LISAs; Enoch researching | **Medium–High** |
| APR and the real cost of borrowing | Avoid traps | Mohammad's credit card situation; debt trap prevention | **Medium–High** |
| Emergency fund — what it is and why you need one | Not feel out of depth | Grace "ran out of money first year"; "I want to always have something I can rely on" | **Medium** |
| Managing variable income | Not feel out of depth | Tutoring income (Mia, Althea); student finance disbursements (Grace); part-time bar work | **Medium** |
| Tax basics — income tax and NI explained | Not feel out of depth | Mohammad (first job); Mia (in tax sector but personal gaps remain); real-life context | **Medium** |
| Understanding student finance | Not feel out of depth | Grace managing 3 disbursements + bar work + parental top-up; debt vs. investment framing | **Medium** |

**Suggested lessons for Young Adults:**
- *Compound interest: why starting early actually makes a difference* — Real numbers; visual comparison; YA-appropriate time horizons (5–10 years, not 40)
- *Which savings account should you actually use?* — Current vs. savings vs. ISA vs. LISA; how to choose; what each one costs you if you don't use it
- *Your credit card is not free money — here's the proof* — APR; minimum payments; the Mohammad scenario; autopay settings
- *What is a credit score and how do you build one?* — Score components; what damages it; early actions that help
- *How to budget when your income isn't the same every month* — Framework for variable income; floor-and-flex method; real scenarios
- *The 0% trap: how promotional rates catch people out* — Timeline mechanics; what "0% for 12 months" actually means
- *How to start investing when you have almost nothing* — ISAs, index funds, low-cost starting points; honest risk framing
- *What is an ISA — and why does it matter?* — Tax-free savings explained; LISA for first-time buyers; decision tree
- *Emergency fund: how much, where to keep it, when to use it* — Real-life trigger examples; recommended size; accessible account types
- *Your first payslip — decoded* — Income tax, NI, pension contributions; net vs. gross; understanding deductions
- *Student loans: debt or investment?* — Repayment mechanics; income-contingent nature; when (if ever) to repay early

**What to exclude at YA level:** Basic concepts that feel patronising (simple needs vs. wants, what is a bank account). These can appear as revision references but should not be primary content. YA participants were explicit that oversimplification undermines trust. Scenarios should use realistic income levels based on age-specific national averages (the research stimuli contained amounts that were flagged as unrealistically high).

## 4.3 Framing Topics in a Relevant Way

Topic selection determines relevance. Framing determines whether users feel it. Three principles emerged consistently across segments:

**1. Anchor every lesson to a situation the user is already in — or will be in very soon.** Abstract concepts fail; concrete scenarios land. Young Teens respond to small amounts and near-term goals. Older Teens want preparation framing ("should you be investing at your age?"), not theory. Young Adults need content that reflects real conditions — irregular income, multiple income streams — not ideal-world assumptions.

**2. Match the emotional weight of the framing to the stakes.** Playful works for Young Teens, where financial stakes are genuinely small. Older Teens are at the edge of that tolerance — they want to be taken seriously. Young Adults are explicit: the right register is a capable, direct peer, not a cheerful mascot.

**3. Use the language they already use — not the language of financial education.** Young Teens understand "making money last" but not "budgeting." Older Teens use Revolut, savings pots, and cashback naturally — explaining these is an engagement killer. Young Adults want precision: *"you know interest exists — here's how to actually use it"* respects existing knowledge while filling the gap.
