# 9. Gamification: Engagement, Motivation, and Momentum

- TOC
{:toc}

## 9.1 What Gamification Features Are in the App

Multiple participants across all three segments compared the app to Duolingo without prompting. The gamification architecture has four elements: **monster collection**, where completing a lesson unlocks a named character (Sammy Savings, Tracy Tracking, etc.) representing a financial theme; **a progression map** with locked and unlocked lessons arranged in a visual path; **streaks** that track consecutive-day usage, making continuity its own reward; and **character guidance** through Mr. Money and lesson-specific characters, which provide pedagogical scaffolding and have headroom to be gamified further.

## 9.2 What's Working
{include following as 3 headers on a single slide}

### 9.2.1 Monster collection: incentive that lands with Young Teens

For Young Teens, monster collection provides a concrete reward structure — an object to acquire, a collection to build — that translates lesson completion into a tangible outcome. Sam (14): *"makes it seem like you're working towards something."* The mechanic is legible because Young Teens are habituated to collecting digital items, and the monsters are distinct named characters rather than generic badges, giving the collection texture beyond points accumulation. The appeal has some cross-segment reach — Enoch (18) found it a *"good incentive alongside streaks"* and Mia (25) described loving the system — but it is at Young Teen level that the mechanic functions most reliably as a motivator without complication.

### 9.2.2 Streaks: effective baseline mechanic across Young Teens and some Older Teens

The streak mechanic is broadly understood and appreciated across the youngest two segments. Lottie (13) identified it as a primary retention mechanism with competitive potential among friends; Ovie (15) said she *"would use daily if got notifications and could maintain streaks"*; even Mohammad (23), despite broader reservations, acknowledged it *"keeps you coming back."*

### 9.2.3 Progress and progression maps: understood and motivating

The progression map — a visual path with locked and unlocked states — communicates structure, progress, and aspiration simultaneously. Enoch (18) understood it as part of a coherent gamified structure; Zain (20) appreciated the *"bite-sized, unlockable chunk layout"* as making the app feel manageable. Crucially, locked content functions as aspiration rather than frustration: participants consistently noticed what was locked and used it to orient themselves, creating a forward-looking pull that the current design handles competently.

## 9.3 What's Missing or Underdeveloped
{include following as headers on a single slide}

### 9.3.1 Expanding gamification: a clear expectation from all segments

The single most consistent finding across all three segments is that the current gamification layer is insufficient. Once participants see a Duolingo-style progression map, they import the full mental model and find the current implementation shallow against that benchmark. 

The desire takes different forms by segment — deeper interactive gameplay for Young Teens, XP with jeopardy for Older Teens (Enoch, 18), content and leaderboard access unlocked through engagement for Young Adults (Zain, 20) — but the common thread is the same: completion without making completion feel sufficiently valuable.

### 9.3.2 Consequence-based gamification: desire for more purpose in collecting monsters

The monster collection system has a specific structural problem: it delivers a reward without explaining what the reward is for. Monsters are earned — but once collected, they do nothing. Althea (19) named this most precisely: *"Monster Collected"* felt like a gimmick because *"she didn't understand where it was leading"* — she wanted gamification that *"builds toward something meaningful, not just awards trophies."* Zain (20) approached the same gap from a mechanics angle: he expected monsters to *"unlock app features, avatar customisations, or leaderboard abilities."* 

Whether the second layer means trading, upgrading, or content-gating matters less than the principle: collection without consequence is a motivation that exhausts itself quickly.

### 9.3.3 Social and competitive features: leaderboards and peer comparison

The social layer is effectively absent, and participants across all three segments flagged it as a significant gap. Levi (17) wanted *"competitive elements with friends — comparing streaks, earning more monsters"*; Ovie (15) proposed *"a competitive element to collect more than friends"*; Zain (20) requested *"public leaderboards."* Levi made the dependency explicit: *"If other people use it, I would definitely start using it"* — social proof is not merely a feature preference but a description of the adoption mechanism. Without a social layer, the retention burden falls entirely on content and individual mechanics: a harder load to carry.

## 9.4 Where Gamification Fails
{include following as 2 headers on a single slide}

### 9.4.1 Young Teens: desire for greater gameplay depth

The gamification failure for Young Teens is not rejection but disappointment: this is the segment most aligned with the monster-collection model, yet the current layer is insufficient for their expectations. Isaac (12) — enthusiastic about the concept and generous in his rating — generated the most pointed critique: gameplay should be *the priority*, with spendable points, monster trading and swapping, and map-based mini-games where financial decisions have navigable consequences. Lottie (13) arrived at a similar conclusion from a different direction: the text-heavy format places too much weight on mechanics that aren't deep enough to carry it, in direct competition with TikTok and Instagram. The risk is that Young Teens engage briefly, find the game layer too thin, and do not return — not because they disliked the concept, but because the execution didn't match the proposition.

### 9.4.2 Older Teens: aesthetic registers younger than the audience

For Older Teens, the primary failure is aesthetic: the visual framing reads younger than the segment it is targeting. Aiden (15) was the hardest case — he immediately perceived the app as *"designed for primary school children"* and would not continue in current form. Ovie (15) was more constructive: she wanted the collecting impulse preserved but the surface changed — *"replace monsters with badges or more age-appropriate rewards"* — cleanly separating mechanics (valued) from aesthetics (rejected). Levi (17), more tolerant of the framing, still noted the design *"feels targeted at 12–14 rather than 16–18."* The fix is an age-up that preserves the motivational architecture without compromising audience credibility.

### 9.4.3 Young Adults: narrower appeal — and that is okay with the right positioning

The current gamification style will not resonate with most Young Adults, and the honest response to that finding is segmentation rather than alarm. Grace (19) rated the app 4/10 and led with *"childish"* and *"too playful"*: the game elements were *"inappropriate for a 19-year-old"* and blocked serious engagement with financial content. Mohammad (23) was more analytically precise in his rejection: the gamification communicated an assumption he found patronising — *"feels like you're just taking them, putting them into a child seat."* Althea (19) identified the subtler version of the same problem: not that gamification is wrong in principle, but that the current implementation is decorative rather than purposeful — *"there should be a degree of seriousness — it's an important topic."*

**The segment is not monolithic:** Enoch (18) was broadly positive, and Mia (25) — who spontaneously referenced Plum, a goal-tracking app with over four million users built on a similarly warm and character-driven aesthetic — described loving the collection system. Plum's scale is a useful reminder that the older end of this market is not categorically hostile to playful, visually expressive design when the underlying utility is evident. What tends to tip the balance is confidence of execution: charm that reads as a deliberate choice rather than a developmental constraint.

The recommendation is not to chase broad Young Adult appeal, which would require compromising the product's core identity, but to position precisely within it. A Money Monsters that leans into its personality with more intent — purposeful gamification, cuteness as a feature rather than an apology — can find a genuine audience within this cohort. It will simply be a smaller slice, and the Money Monsters should be comfortable owning that.
