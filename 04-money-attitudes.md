## 4. Money in their life — income, spending, saving

- **Young Teens:** Income from pocket money (£5–15/week or fortnight), birthday cash, and occasional chores. Most spend quickly after receiving. Impulse control is the main challenge — not budgeting. Some have saved toward specific goals (e.g., a PC over two years; football boots), showing motivation-based saving is achievable when tied to something concrete.
- **Older Teens:** Starting to earn real money — e.g., hourly wages from events work, selling vintage clothes online, paper rounds, or actively seeking a first part-time role. Goal-directed saving is happening: saving for a car, a school bag, specific experiences. But impulse tension remains the central challenge — several described knowing they should save while admitting they struggle to follow through.
- **Young Adults:** Real complexity. Some live independently on near-minimum wage managing rent; others balance student loans, part-time work, and parental support. One participant owns a home and has a mortgage; another lives paycheck to paycheck. Stakes are real and consequential.
## How they manage their money

- **Young Teens:** Child-specific accounts (e.g. Monzo, NatWest Rooster) or cash (especially for 12–13 year olds). "Managing" means checking a balance and self-regulating spending.
- **Older Teens:** Accounts range from basic current accounts (Nationwide, NatWest Adapt) to digital-first tools (Revolut, Monzo, Santander). The more financially active participants had developed informal account architecture as a substitute for discipline: The variation within this group is striking: some are already designing their own financial systems; others haven't started.
- **Young Adults:** Widest range — and most dissatisfied with their current systems. Some track mentally with no system; others use spreadsheets for major decisions or split money across multiple accounts to enforce separation (e.g., bills vs. discretionary). Some use passive savings apps (e.g., Plum) but found them too easy to override. 
  
  **Consistent theme:** existing tools are too manual, too passive, or too easy to circumvent.

>Ariana (17) deliberately split money across three accounts — Santander for day-to-day spending, Monzo for longer-term savings, and PayPal for online purchases. 
>Ovie (15) used Revolut's automatic weekly saving sweep to move money without having to consciously decide. 

Both reflect an instinct to engineer separation — removing the temptation by making savings slightly harder to reach. Others hadn't reached this point: 

>Levi (17) relied on a single account and mental monitoring of his balance; Aiden (15) had a savings account set up by his parents but couldn't access it — savings infrastructure imposed from outside rather than self-managed.

## What They Know About Money

### Where they learned about money

- **Family first** — majority of participants are the dominant channel across all segments: saving habits, investing awareness, understanding what's possible. For YA's this extends to wider family and friends.
- **In the UK school is consistently described as inadequate** — too academic, too basic, or simply absent. e.g., described as "more like economics than actual money"; school lessons on bank accounts that simply repeated what parents had already taught.
- **CZ – Compulsory education**– since 2009 in secondary schools, 2013 in primary. Predominantly taught in primary schools in mathematics, civics, and social sciences though some respondents have it as separate courses and financial literacy competitions.**
- **Self-directed digital learning** grows with age — e.g., learning from TikTok and AI; researching intensively when a situation demands it; following Martin Lewis and Money Saving Expert. Quality and relevance vary — YouTube content often assumes income levels young people don't have.
### What they wish they understood better 

*NB: Explored before research stimuli was shown*

- **Young Teens:** **GENERAL KNOWLEDGE**
  Adult concepts they overhear — e.g., mortgages (prompted by a parent's house move), currency exchange (ahead of foreign travel). Not urgent financial need — more about not feeling left out of conversations.
- **Older Teens:** **SPECIFIC SKILLS**
  Practical knowledge for decisions approaching in 1–3 years — e.g., housing and mortgages, whether to invest now, how cashback works, taxes.
- **Young Adults:** **REAL-TIME DECISION MAKING**
  Mechanics behind products they already hold or decisions they're actively making — e.g., how credit card minimum payments and autopay defaults work, ISA and compound interest gaps, how LISAs work for first-time buyers. These aren't abstract — they're live and consequential.
### Confidence and knowledge self-rating

- **Young Teens:** Understand basics (saving, spending) but have no real knowledge of financial products, interest, or what financial life looks like later. Financial confidence at this age is often relative and socially constructed. Typical self-rating: 5–7/10.
- **Older Teens:** Know the saving principles but admit execution is the weak point. The real gap is confidence in adult financial conversations, not day-to-day mechanics. Typical self-rating: 6–7/10.
- **Young Adults:** The most variable group — and the most revealing. Some rate themselves as highly competent while making costly product mistakes that show confidence outrunning understanding (e.g., paying avoidable credit card interest). Self-ratings range 4–8/10, with emotional state often a better predictor of behavior than score.

> Marie (14, CZ) rated herself a confident 10/10 simply because she doesn't buy energy drinks like her classmates. 
> In CZ, Jan (16) rated himself 9/10 for managing money well — but couldn't define “interest” when it appeared in the lesson.

## Key Insight: The Confidence Trap

Two compounding patterns emerged consistently across both markets which helps explain why users won't learn what they think they already know.

### 1. The Confidence–Competence Gap

Young people rate themselves as financially confident — but that confidence is largely reflecting one narrow skill: saving or showing restraint. Understanding of actual financial mechanics (interest, credit, risk) falls well short of what self-assessment suggests.

- **This is Dunning-Kruger in action.** They don't know enough to recognize what they don't know
- **Present in both markets, particularly in CZ** — participants who rated themselves confident couldn't define key terms or explain how common financial products work

> *"It was quite simple, so I knew what I was reading."* — Levi (16, UK), moments before admitting he didn't know what the 50/30/20 rule was — the very framework he'd just been shown.

> Ládislav (13, CZ) rated himself above average because he doesn't buy *"stupid things"* right away. When asked if he discusses saving money with his friends, he explicitly noted: *"We don't talk about that at all... [The only time they discuss money is] when we put the money together to buy something together"*

**How this affects Money Monsters**

- Users who think they already understand money won't seek financial education.
- The onboarding baseline check must do more than assess — it must *reveal the gap*.
- Show users what they think they know vs. what they actually know. Done well, this creates curiosity, not shame. ("Huh, I thought I knew that.") Done badly — or skipped — the most confident users bounce first.

### 2. The Intention–Action Gap

Even where genuine understanding exists, applying it in the moment of a financial decision is a different cognitive task. This is the **intention–action gap** — well-documented in health psychology.

- **The healthy-eating parallel is exact:** from a young age we know how nutrition works and will still reach for sweets. Knowledge doesn't automatically drive behaviour in context
- **This is not about young people "failing"** to do the right thing. What to do with money is subjective, values-driven, and contextual. A 16-year-old spending all their earnings on something they love isn't making a mistake — they're making a trade-off.

> *"I see money and I want to spend it."* — Aiden (15, UK)

> *"Money just doesn't really come into my head when I think about the future that much... I'm very prone to just spending."* — Dulcie (16, UK), who also admitted she'd "probably start [the app] and then just not want to do it because it's based around money."

**Money Monster's role:** build knowledge, skill, and habits that make trade-offs *informed* — not moralise about which choices are correct
### How these two gaps compound

- Inflated confidence kills motivation to learn
- Without deeper understanding, the intention–action gap can't close — you can't apply knowledge you don't actually have
- The result: users who are simultaneously overconfident *and* under-equipped

**What this means for Money Monsters:**

- **Onboarding must crack the confidence shell.** The baseline knowledge check shouldn't just score users — it should surface the gap. A participant who rates themselves 8/10 but can't explain what APR stands for has just had a productive "huh" moment. That's the hook. Curiosity, not shame. Skip this calibration step and the most confident users — the ones who need it most — bounce first.
- **Content must teach mechanics, not morals.** Mohammad didn't know his credit card autopay defaulted to minimum — that's a mechanics gap, not a values failure. Grace didn't understand compound interest until the app demo showed her. Zain finds ISA content "too generic to be actionable." The content that lands explains *how money actually behaves* — not what users should do with it. "Here's how interest compounds against you" works. "You should be saving more" doesn't.
- **Scenario-based rehearsal closes the intention–action gap.** Knowing how credit works and making a good call in the moment are different cognitive tasks. The app's "help this person decide" format is exactly the right vehicle — it lets users practise financial reasoning in realistic contexts without the stakes of real money. Lottie's impulse spending, Mohammad's autopay mistake, Grace running out of money at university — these are the scenarios that build decision-making muscle through repetition, not lecture.

## Learning Styles of Young People

### Introduction to learning styles

How these young people learn matters as much as what they need to know. Three well-established theories from learning science help explain what we observed — and why a single approach won't work across all three segments.

- **Motivation shifts with age.** Younger teens are driven by rewards and social comparison; older teens by personal relevance; young adults by immediate, real-world need. Gamification that works for one group will feel patronizing to another.¹
- **Working memory has limits.** When information arrives too densely or too abstractly, learners disengage before anything sticks — and this effect is strongest where knowledge is thinnest.² Format matters as much as content.
- **Real situations make learning stick.** Learning attached to authentic decisions — real money, real consequences — is retained far better than abstract instruction, at every age.³

Together, these explain why what motivates engagement, how much depth is tolerable, and what format works all shift predictably across our three segments.

*¹ Self-Determination Theory — Deci & Ryan, 1985 · ² Cognitive Load Theory — Sweller, 1988 · ³ Situated Learning Theory — Lave & Wenger, 1991*

|                           | **Young Teens**                    | **Older Teens**                                 | **Young Adults**                                                         |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **What motivates them**   | Reward, social comparison          | Relevance to emerging identity                  | Real need, real consequence                                              |
| **What hooks them**       | Play, curiosity, short discovery   | Near-future decisions, practical use            | Current situation, lived experience                                      |

| **Format that works**     | Short, visual, interactive, social | Contextual, scenario-based, utility-based       | Specificity, depth (with relevance),<br>actionable utility or simulation |

- Session dip: ~5–8 min — keep modules at 3–5 min each; reward loops drive another round rather than an exit
