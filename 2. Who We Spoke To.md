## 2.1 Participant Overview

- **UK:** x16, virtual 60-minute sessions, 
- **Czechia:** x16 participants, same segment split

- All sessions were screen-share and think-aloud format
- Recruited to be mainstream — not finance enthusiasts
- Deliberately skewed toward lower financial literacy and lower household income
- Excluded: finance-industry households and anyone with formal financial training
- Most had minimal or no useful financial education at school

| Segment           | n   | Ages  | Profile                                                             |
| ----------------- | --- | ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Young Teens (YT)  | 7   | 12–14 | Pocket money; no financial independence                             |
| Older Teens (OT)  | 3   | 15–17 | First earnings; approaching key life decisions                      |
| Young Adults (YA) | 6   | 18–25 | Early employment or higher education; real financial responsibility |

## 2.2 Participant Context and Money Habits

### 2.2.1 Money in their life — income, spending, saving

- **Young Teens:** Income from pocket money (£5–15/week or fortnight), birthday cash, and occasional chores. Most spend quickly after receiving. Impulse control is the main challenge — not budgeting. Some have saved toward specific goals (e.g., a PC over two years; football boots), showing motivation-based saving is achievable when tied to something concrete.
- **Older Teens:** Starting to earn real money — e.g., hourly wages from events work, selling vintage clothes online, or actively seeking a first part-time role. Goal-directed saving is happening, but impulse tension remains.
- **Young Adults:** Real complexity. Some live independently on near-minimum wage managing rent; others balance student loans, part-time work, and parental support. One participant owns a home and has a mortgage; another lives paycheck to paycheck. Stakes are real and consequential.

### 2.2.2 How they manage their money

- **Young Teens:** Child-specific accounts (e.g. Monzo, NatWest Rooster) or cash (especially for 12–13 year olds). "Managing" means checking a balance and self-regulating spending.
- **Older Teens:** Standard or teen current accounts (e.g. Revolut). Check balances regularly but no formal budgets. One participant used an automatic saving sweep — the closest to structured saving behaviour in this group.
- **Young Adults:** Widest range — and most dissatisfied with their current systems. Some track mentally with no system; others use spreadsheets for major decisions or split money across multiple accounts to enforce separation (e.g., bills vs. discretionary). Some use passive savings apps (e.g., Plum) but find them too easy to override. Consistent theme: existing tools are too manual, too passive, or too easy to circumvent.

## 2.3 What They Know About Money

### 2.3.1 Confidence and knowledge self-rating

- **Young Teens:** Understand basics (saving, spending) but have no real knowledge of financial products, interest, or what financial life looks like later. Financial confidence at this age is often relative and socially constructed — one outlier rated themselves very highly because peers "know nothing." Typical self-rating: 5–7/10.
- **Older Teens:** Know the saving principles but admit execution is the weak point. The real gap is confidence in adult financial conversations, not day-to-day mechanics. Typical self-rating: 6–7/10.
- **Young Adults:** The most variable group — and the most revealing. Some rate themselves as highly competent while making costly product mistakes that show confidence outrunning understanding (e.g., paying avoidable credit card interest). Others describe a near-helpless relationship with money. Self-ratings range 4–8/10, with emotional state often a better predictor of behaviour than score.

### 2.3.2 Where they learned about money

- **Family first** — almost universal. Parents are the dominant channel across all segments: saving habits, investing awareness, understanding what's possible. For Young Adults this extends to wider family.
- **School is consistently described as inadequate** — too academic, too basic, or simply absent. e.g., described as "more like economics than actual finance"; school lessons on bank accounts that simply repeated what parents had already taught.
- **Self-directed digital learning** grows with age — e.g., learning from TikTok and AI; researching intensively when a situation demands it; following Martin Lewis and Money Saving Expert. Quality and relevance vary — YouTube content often assumes income levels young people don't have.

### 2.3.3 What they wish they understood better

- **Young Teens:** Adult concepts they overhear — e.g., mortgages (prompted by a parent's house move), currency exchange (ahead of foreign travel). Not urgent financial need — more about not feeling left out of conversations.
- **Older Teens:** Practical knowledge for decisions approaching in 1–3 years — e.g., housing and mortgages, whether to invest now, how cashback works, taxes.
- **Young Adults:** Mechanics behind products they already hold or decisions they're actively making — e.g., how credit card minimum payments and autopay defaults work, ISA and compound interest gaps, how LISAs work for first-time buyers. These aren't abstract — they're live and consequential.

## 2.4 Learning Styles of Young People

### 2.4.1 Introduction to learning styles

How these young people learn matters as much as what they need to know. Three well-established theories from learning science help explain what we observed — and why a single approach won't work across all three segments.

- **Motivation shifts with age.** Younger teens are driven by rewards and social comparison; older teens by personal relevance; young adults by immediate, real-world need. Gamification that works for one group will feel patronizing to another.¹
- **Working memory has limits.** When information arrives too densely or too abstractly, learners disengage before anything sticks — and this effect is strongest where knowledge is thinnest.² Format matters as much as content.
- **Real situations make learning stick.** Learning attached to authentic decisions — real money, real consequences — is retained far better than abstract instruction, at every age.³

Together, these explain why what motivates engagement, how much depth is tolerable, and what format works all shift predictably across our three segments.

*¹ Self-Determination Theory — Deci & Ryan, 1985 · ² Cognitive Load Theory — Sweller, 1988 · ³ Situated Learning Theory — Lave & Wenger, 1991*

|                         | **Young Teens**                    | **Older Teens**                                 | **Young Adults**                                              |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **What motivates them** | Reward, social comparison          | Relevance to emerging identity                  | Real need, real consequence                                   |
| **What hooks them**     | Play, curiosity, short discovery   | Near-future decisions, practical use            | Current situation, lived experience                           |
| **Format that works**   | Short, visual, interactive, social | Contextual, scenario-based, adult-toned         | Specificity, depth (with relevance),<br>actionable simulation |
| **Risk of losing them** | Boredom — feels like school        | Condescension — feels designed for younger kids | Superficiality — not deep enough to be useful                 |

### 2.4.2 Learning styles mapped by our three segments

**Young Teens — learn through play, social comparison, and doing**

This group is actively curious, but that curiosity needs to be activated and sustained. Content that looks or feels like school gets tuned out fast. Short, visual, interactive, and competitive formats work best — and social comparison is a genuine motivator, not just a nice-to-have.

*Examples:* Sam abandoned earlier finance apps that were "way too hard" or "really boring" — he actively looks for game-like formats. Hugo's strongest learning moments were curiosity-driven: researching why a snack got more expensive, watching TikTok about how kids make money. Zain (13) wants to "get a head start" on peers.

*Contrast:* Isaac (12) shows less spontaneous curiosity — his learning is more passive and shaped almost entirely by family conversations. For him, social comparison features may matter less than relatable, practical scenarios.

---

**Older Teens — learn through relevance and growing financial identity**

This group is forming a sense of who they are with money. They don't respond as readily to gamification as younger teens do — what lands is content that connects directly to situations they're already in or can clearly see coming.

*Examples:* Levi (17) engaged immediately with the 50/30/20 rule because he could apply it to his own earnings. Aiden (15) wants role-play and practical scenarios over abstract lessons. Both flagged the app's visual design as "primary school"-level — this group has a higher aesthetic threshold than Young Teens.

*Contrast:* Ovie (15) is already self-directing her learning — researching how Revolut's savings features work and whether she should be investing. She's behaving more like a Young Adult learner and would benefit from access to more depth than the current content offers.

---

**Young Adults — learn through immediate need and situation-specific depth**

This group learns best when content maps directly onto something they're already living. Abstract or surface-level content doesn't just fail to help — it actively frustrates.

*Examples:* Mohammad (23) found a lesson on credit card minimum payments immediately relevant because he was already living the consequences of getting it wrong. Grace (19) retained what she learned about compound interest because it connected to her own student savings question during the session.

*Contrast:* Mohammad and Zain both wanted content to go further — Mohammad wanted 2–3 minute sessions rather than quick bite-sizes; Zain felt content was "scraping the top of things." But Althea's need was different: she didn't want more information in general — she wanted to know specifically what *she* should do. More depth isn't always the answer; more personalisation sometimes matters more.
