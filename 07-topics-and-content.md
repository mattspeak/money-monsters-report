## Introduction

This section translates research findings into a content roadmap — mapping what exists, what needs to be added, and what to prioritise by segment. It draws on three inputs: what participants said they wanted to learn, what their behaviours and anxieties implied they needed, and the JTBD priorities confirmed in [[5. What Do Young People Actually Want from Financial Education?|§5]]. 

Content is weighted toward the segments established in [[6. Segment Verdicts|§6]]: V1 Bullseye Young Teens (14–16), V2 Target Older Teens (17–19) and briefly covering Young Adults (20–25).

---

## Effective Learning Requires a Balance of Knowledge, Skills, and Habits

[[4. Money Attitudes, Behaviors and Habits]] has established that financial literacy is not a single dimension. It requires three interconnected pillars:

- **Knowledge** — understanding concepts: e.g. what interest is, how credit scores work
- **Skills** — applying knowledge in context: e.g. comparing savings accounts, making a spending decision
- **Habits** — repeating the right behaviours automatically: checking before buying, saving regularly, reviewing spending

The app currently leans heavily into knowledge delivery — but knowledge alone does not change behaviour (see [[4. Money Attitudes, Behaviors and Habits|§4: The Confidence Trap]]).

**Where the Money Monsters sits today vs. where it needs to move:**

|                                | Knowledge | Skills | Habits |
| ------------------------------ | --------- | ------ | ------ |
| **Proposed learning strategy** | ●●○       | ●○○    | ○○○    |
| **V1 Target**                  | ●●●       | ●●○    | ●○○    |
| **V2 Target**                  | ●●●       | ●●●    | ●●○    |

**What this means in practice:**

- **Knowledge:** The foundation is strong — focus lesson development to the target segment
- **Skills:** Expand scenario-based practice and branching decisions — the "help this person decide" format is the right vehicle and the V1 priority
- **Habits:** Beyond lesson nudges, genuine habit forming functionality will require significant investment (e.g. built in utility; e.g. saving goals etc.); work towards building genuine behavioural loops in V2. See [[15. Prioritised Recommendations]]

## V1 Bullseye: Young Teens (14–16)

Young Teens are forming their first real relationship with money — income is shifting from occasional pocket money to live spending decisions, and financial concepts are genuinely novel rather than a recap of things they already manage. 

Key for tables: 'Existing' refers to likely content to work with from existing resources; 'New ➕' refers to new content required.

---
### ●●● Manage My Money Wisely / Make It Last

The core tension is impulse control — spending with friends, small habitual purchases — rather than abstract budgeting. Most Young Teens described spending money quickly after receiving it.

> *"I can't save money. I just spend it."* — Olivia (14, UK)
> Zain (13, UK) spent £11 at a shop *"without thinking"* while out with friends.

**Topics serving this job:**

| Topic                                            | Status              | App Version | Remarks                                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------- | ----------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Needs vs. wants                                  | Existing            | **V1**      | Clothes impulse buys (Olivia, Lottie); "without thinking" spending confirmed by Zain                                                           |
| Saving basics and goal-setting                   | Existing            | **V1**      | Console goal (Sam, 2 years); football boots (Isaac); Korean trip (Jaroslav, CZ)                                                                |
| Micro-budgeting / making pocket money go further | Existing; to evolve | **V1**      | Small amounts, short time horizons; practical tips and skill building. L4 covers general budgeting; the pocket-money scale and framing is new. |
| Resisting impulse spending                       | **New ➕**           | **V2**      | Whilst most YT participants describe the need this may be challenging to build a habit without considerable tech implimentation                |

**Suggested lessons example framing**

- *What's the difference between saving and spending?* — Needs vs. wants; mental frameworks for real choices
- *Save for the thing you actually want* — Goal-setting mechanics; splitting money; what happens to it over time
- *Make your pocket money go further* — Practical mini-budgeting; a week in the life
- *Why does my money keep disappearing?* — The lowdown on impulse spending; why it happens; simple tricks to slow it down

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"Spend it or save it?"** — A branching scenario following a character (e.g. Zain) who has £20 of pocket money. Players face three sequential decisions about spending with friends; each choice reveals the downstream consequence. Multiple-choice with short explanatory feedback after each decision.
- **"Pocket money planner"** — A micro-budgeting challenge: allocate a fixed weekly amount across five real spending categories (food, transport, fun, saving, other). A visual dial shows how far the money stretches; wrong distributions trigger a visible shortfall moment. Drag-and-drop allocation modality.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- Partner with Rooster or GoHenry to push a goal-progress reminder at the point of purchase — so the teen sees how close they are to their goal before they decide to spend.
- Generate a downloadable lock-screen image showing the user's goal name and amount remaining — passive front-of-mind reinforcement every time they pick up their phone.

### ●●● Save Toward a Meaningful Goal

Every participant had a recent example of goal-directed saving — a gaming console, a PC, trainers, a trip — all concrete, personal, and near-term. Abstract lessons about long-term financial futures land poorly. What works is connecting concepts to something the participant is already motivated by.

> *"Right now I save for new equipment for refereeing and then I want to save for a new mobile phone."* — Ladislav (13, CZ)
> *"I would like to go to Korea sometime, so I would save for a plane ticket."* — Jaroslav (14, CZ)

**Topics serving this job:**

| Topic                                               | Status    | App Version | Remarks                                                                                                        |
| --------------------------------------------------- | --------- | ----------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Goal-setting and saving milestones                  | Existing  | **V1**      | Console goal (Sam, 2 years); football boots (Isaac); concrete goal cited by every YT participant               |
| What is interest? (savings as reward for waiting)   | Existing  | **V1**      | Conceptual intro; foundation for OT savings content                                                            |
| Goals-based savings: how much do you actually need? | Existing; to evolve | **V1**      | L4.7 covers SMART financial goals; this evolves that into concrete numbers and aspiration-anchored planning rather than abstract frameworks. |


**Suggested lessons example framing**

- *How long until you can afford it?* — Pick your goal, work out the numbers, track progress
- *What does interest mean?* — First introduction; savings interest as a reward for waiting
- *The travel fund challenge* — If you want to go somewhere at 18, what do you need to start now?

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"How long until you have it?"** — Players see a character's goal (e.g. Sam's console at £300) and a weekly saving figure; a visual countdown updates in real time. A follow-up multiple-choice challenge asks which savings habit — same amount weekly, occasional top-ups, or spending first — gets Sam there fastest, with feedback explaining the difference.
- **"Interest: your money's invisible helper"** — A sequence of true/false and fill-in-the-blank questions built around a simple savings scenario. A side-by-side visual shows the same amount left in a drawer vs. put in a savings account over 12 months; players identify which strategy earns more and why, then predict the gap.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- Add a shareable goal-tracker card — a simple savings thermometer users can save to their camera roll — so progress is visible outside the app and doubles as a social sharing moment.
- Send a weekly push notification anchored to goal progress ("You're £12 closer to your console this week") — a small, specific moment of positive reinforcement that rewards patience over impulse spending.

### ●●○ Feel Confident / Not Stressed or Embarrassed About Money

Unlike Older Teens and Young Adults, where this job centres on readiness for independence, for Young Teens the emotional territory is social: not wanting to look stupid in conversations, not wanting to be caught pretending to understand something they don't.

> *"I don't want to feel embarrassed when money is discussed because I don't understand it."* — Franta (13, CZ)
> *"I don't want to feel silly when money comes up in conversations."* — Isaac (12, UK)
> Ladislav (13, CZ) described a habit of pretending to understand when unsure.

**Topics serving this job:**

| Topic                                                 | Status    | App Version | Remarks                                                                                                   |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | --------- | ----------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Bank Accounts 101                                     | Existing  | **V1**      | Uses Rooster (Norah); piggy bank vs. bank account (Isaac); "I check mine daily" vs. "I can't access mine" |
| Understanding adults talking about money              | **New ➕** | **V1**      | Mortgages (Isaac, after parents' house move); taxes (Norah); investing (Hugo, after dad's conversations)  |
| What is investing? (demystifying, no action required) | Existing; to evolve | **V2**      | L8 is an 8-lesson investing suite; this evolves it into a single demystifying lesson appropriate for YT, with no action required. |

**Suggested lessons example framing**

- *What even is a bank account?* — Account types explained simply; why savings pots matter; how banks hold money
- *Adults keep saying "invest" — what does that mean?* — Demystifying the concept; no action required at this age
- *Words you'll hear adults use about money* — Mortgages, taxes, interest, credit — a decoder, not a curriculum

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"Decode the money conversation"** — Situation: Isaac's parents are talking about their mortgage. He overhears four financial terms. Players match each term to a plain-language meaning; a final multiple-choice question asks what Isaac could say to sound confident joining the conversation. Confirms vocabulary retention without requiring any financial action.
- **"What kind of account is that?"** — A swipe-to-sort challenge: six account types (piggy bank, current account, savings account, Monzo pot, Rooster card, ISA) sorted into "safe for now" vs. "makes money grow." Introduces account types through recognition rather than recall.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- Send a "money word of the week" push notification — one term, one tap to a micro-lesson — building financial vocabulary through repeated low-friction exposure rather than relying on a single lesson to do the work.
- Surface a "money decoder" shortcut users can add to their phone home screen, so looking up an unfamiliar term when it comes up in conversation is faster than pretending to understand it.

> **Note on earning money:** Whilst several YT participants sell items, do chores, or expressed curiosity about generating income, we do not recommend that a financial literacy product actively encourage children under 16 to seek earned income. Where earning does arise in lessons, the responsible framing is around managing what you already have — not promoting earning as a solution. Earning-related content appears from the Older Teen segment onwards.

## V2 Target: Older Teens (17–19)

Older Teens occupy a transitional zone. They are earning real income (part-time jobs, zero-hours contracts), approaching significant financial decisions, and more aware of the financial world — and its risks. Content should bridge their current reality and the transition ahead.

The V2 upper limit is **19, not 18** — in the Czech Republic, 19 is the year students typically leave full-time education and enter financial independence, making it the natural top of this cohort.

---

### ●●● Manage My Money Wisely / Make It Last

The shift from Young Teens is clear: this is no longer about pocket money. These respondents have part-time earnings, zero-hours contracts, Vinted income — and the job is to make those real earnings count.

> Levi (17, UK) earns £12/hour on a zero-hours contract and wants to ensure it goes on useful things.
> Aiden (15, UK) frames smart choices as avoiding decisions that "destroy" his financial position.

**Topics serving this job:**

| Topic                                        | Status              | App Version | Remarks                                                                                                                                                             |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------- | ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Smart spending frameworks (e.g. 50/30/20)    | Existing            | **V2**      | Placed at V2 to match real part-time earnings context; resonated strongly with Aiden and Levi                                                                       |
| Savings account types and how they work      | Existing            | **V2**      | Confusion confirmed across OT: participants actively saving but unsure of account types or rates                                                                    |
| Interest rates — how they affect you         | Existing            | **V2**      | Knowledge gap across all OT participants; foundational for savings and credit decisions ahead                                                                       |
| Compound interest — actual mechanics         | Existing; to evolve | **V2**      | L2.4 mentions compound interest in passing; this evolves it into a standalone topic covering the actual mechanics for 17–19-year-olds choosing savings accounts.    |
| Savings accounts and ISAs                    | Existing            | **V2**      | L5.4 (Specialised savings accounts) and L5.5 (Tax-efficient savings / ISAs) cover this directly; focus is on sequencing and audience-appropriate framing for 17–19. |
| Budgeting for irregular income               | **New ➕**           | **V2**      | Suitable for older teens (17–19) with zero-hours or part-time earnings; framework for variable income; Mia (2 income streams); Grace (student finance + bar work)   |
| Automating savings                           | Existing; to evolve | **V2**      | L4.9 covers automating a budget; this evolves it into savings-specific automation (standing orders, Revolut pots) anchored to OT earners' real behaviour.           |
| First jobs and payslips                      | **New ➕**           | **V2**      | Directly relevant: Levi (£12/hr, zero-hours); approaching first employment for others in cohort                                                                     |
| Earnings potential of different career paths | **New ➕**           | **V2**      | Many OT and YA participants held unrealistic income expectations; lesson grounds aspiration in data                                                                 |

> *"I know you can put money into savings... but what type?"* — Ovie (OT, UK)
> *"I see it on my Revolut but I'm not sure what it means."* — Aiden and Ovie (OT, UK), independently, on cashback

**Suggested lessons example framing**

- *The 50/30/20 rule — does it work for you?* — Practical spending framework; how to adapt it to part-time income
- *Which savings account is actually right for me?* — Account types; why it matters; what interest rate means in practice
- *How interest actually grows (for and against you)* — Interest on savings vs. interest on debt; same mechanic, different direction
- *Compound interest: why starting early actually makes a difference* — Real numbers; visual comparison; 5–10 year time horizons; especially relevant for 17–19-year-olds choosing savings accounts
- *ISAs and savings accounts — what's the difference and which should you open?* — ISAs become actionable at 18; preparation framing for those approaching that threshold
- *How to budget when your income isn't the same every month* — Framework for variable income; floor-and-flex method; real scenarios for zero-hours and part-time earners (17–19)
- *Your first payslip: what all those deductions actually mean* — Tax, NI, pension contributions; real-number examples
- *What do different jobs actually pay?* — Realistic expectations for different career paths; grounding aspiration in data

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"Split Levi's paycheck"** — A 50/30/20 allocation challenge using a realistic zero-hours contract payout (e.g. £180 for two weeks). Players drag income across needs, wants, and savings; the app then shows what happens when the income fluctuates the following month, requiring reallocation. Tests adaptive budgeting, not just formula recall.
- **"Payslip detective"** — Players are shown a realistic first payslip with deductions (NI, income tax, pension). Multiple-choice questions identify what each line means and whether a given deduction is standard or an error. Based on Levi's and similar OT participants' first-employment context.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- Build a one-time "payday setup" walkthrough inside the app that ends with a deep link into Monzo or Revolut's savings pot creation screen — the lesson doesn't just explain pay-yourself-first, it completes the setup before the user exits.
- Add a one-tap "add to calendar" action at lesson end that drops a recurring monthly payday reminder into the phone calendar — prompting the user to move money before discretionary spending starts each month.

### ●●● Protect Myself from Financial Traps and Manipulation

The highest-consensus job across the entire CZ OT group and a top-three priority for the majority of UK OT. These participants are encountering real financial products and real risks for the first time, often without adult supervision.

> Aiden watched £4,000+ of YouTube scam content to understand tactics.
> Enoch lost £100 to a sophisticated Telegram-based job scam requiring staged deposits.
> Dulcie rated scam avoidance as her only Very Important card.

**Topics serving this job:**

| Topic | Status | App Version | Remarks |
| ----- | ------ | ----------- | ------- |
| Scam recognition — how fraud works in practice | **Elevated ↑** | **V2** | Currently buried at L5.7 and L6.8; elevated to a standalone priority; pattern recognition framing over checklist |
| Buy now pay later — what it really costs | **New ➕** | **V2** | OT approaching first exposure to credit products; prevention framing; entirely absent from current curriculum |
| Credit cards — how they actually work | Existing; to evolve | **V2** | L2.7 and L6.2 cover credit cards; this evolves the framing for 17–19-year-olds approaching first products — APR, minimum payments, autopay traps — using real-consequence scenarios. |
| Mythbusting get-rich-quick thinking | **New ➕** | **V2** | Unrealistic income expectations confirmed across OT and YA; Hugo's millionaire-from-Fortnite belief illustrates the gap |

**Suggested lessons example framing**

- *How scams work — and how they get people like you* — Pattern recognition; real tactics; how to respond, not panic
- *Buy now pay later: the small print you need to read* — How promotional rates work; what happens when they expire
- *Your credit card is not free money — here's the proof* — APR; minimum payments; the Mohammad scenario; autopay settings; for older teens (17–19) approaching first credit products
- *Can you really get rich quick?* — Realism and mythbusting; what actually builds wealth vs. what's sold as a shortcut

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"Spot the scam"** — Players are presented with four realistic messages (WhatsApp, Instagram DM, email, job ad). They tap to highlight red flags in each; a score reveals which signals they missed. Based on real tactics from Aiden's and Enoch's experiences — pattern recognition over checklist memorisation.
- **"What does this BNPL offer actually cost?"** — A branching scenario: a player buys a £150 item on "0% for 6 months." Multiple-choice questions track what happens at month 6 if they haven't cleared the balance, and compare the total cost to paying upfront. Surfaces the hidden cost through lived consequence, not explanation.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- End the scam lesson with a shareable "3 red flags" card users can screenshot and keep in their camera roll — the checklist is available in the moment it's needed, not buried in a closed app.
- Signpost to Action Fraud, Get Safe Online, or the Which? Scam Checker at lesson end — the app positions itself as a trusted gateway to the wider safety infrastructure, not a standalone tool.

### ●●● Feel Confident and Not Stressed or Embarrassed About Money

Rated Very Important by 6 of 11 respondents. Beneath the surface, this reflects the coming transition: all OT participants are within 1–3 years of significant financial independence. The horizon is visible and slightly daunting.

> Ariana (17, UK): *"everything spirals"* — even simple financial decisions feel overwhelming and interconnected.
> Levi (17, UK) described his relationship with money as "on edge," particularly in conversations with financially knowledgeable people.

**Topics serving this job:**

| Topic                                                 | Status    | App Version | Remarks                                                                                                                 |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | --------- | ----------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Credit scores — what they are and why they matter now | Existing  | **V2**      | OT approaching first credit products; readiness anxiety confirmed across the group                                      |
| Investing 101 — should you start now?                 | Existing; to evolve | **V2**      | L8 is an 8-lesson investing suite; this evolves it into one well-scoped lesson responding to OT curiosity without action-readiness. L8 significantly overshoots this need. |

> *"My parents invest — should I?"* — Aiden and Ovie (OT, UK)

**Suggested lessons example framing**

- *How does my credit score get built?* — Early credit building; what damages it; why starting now matters
- *Should a 16-year-old invest? (Honest answer)* — Honest framing; what to consider; how to start if the answer is yes

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"What's damaging your credit score?"** — Players review a character's financial behaviour over three months (missed payment, high card utilisation, new account opened). Multiple-choice questions identify the credit score impact of each action; players then advise on the best recovery move. Teaches credit mechanics through consequence, not theory.
- **"Should a 17-year-old invest?"** — A structured consideration challenge: players weigh four factors (emergency fund, existing debt, time horizon, risk tolerance) in a given scenario and make a multiple-choice recommendation. A reveal explains the expert view — validating the reasoning process, not just the answer.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- Add a one-tap "add to calendar" action at lesson end that schedules a monthly "money MOT" reminder — with a deep link back into a short in-app check-in — so the habit is built in from the start rather than left to self-discipline.
- Signpost to ClearScore or Credit Karma (both free, no credit card required) directly from the lesson completion screen — checking their actual score becomes the immediate next step, not a vague future intention.

### Supporting: Save Toward a Goal ●●○ / Prepare for Independence ●●○

**Save toward a meaningful goal** — Meaningful goals have evolved with new income sources and aspirations (cars, gifts, experiences). Scored lower than other OT jobs as participants rated managing money more important than saving for specific goals at this stage.

**Prepare for living independently** — Only 1/12 rated Very Important, but 10/12 rated "kinda important." Independence is not yet a current reality, but the horizon is close enough to register. This is also where the broadest life-stage content sits — topics that stretch beyond conventional financial literacy toward the real decisions this age group is approaching.

| Topic | Status | App Version | Remarks |
| ----- | ------ | ----------- | ------- |
| Goals-based savings for life milestones | Existing | **V2** | Goals have evolved with new income: Honza (16, CZ) saving for a car; travel fund planning (CZ respondents) |
| State benefits — understanding your entitlements | **New ➕** | **V2** | Flagged as especially important; ensuring young people know what they're owed and how to access it |
| Uni, apprenticeship, or straight to work? | **New ➕** | **V2** | Student loan conversations actively happening; a live and consequential question for this age group |
| Ideas for making money — realistic expectations | **New ➕** | **V2** | Curiosity about income generation is strong; frame around realistic returns, not hustle culture |

**Suggested lessons example framing**

- *If you want to travel at 18, how much do you actually need?* — Goals-based planning; connecting aspiration to real numbers
- *What benefits are you entitled to — and how do you claim them?* — Understanding state support; what life looks like on benefits; ensuring young people get what they're owed
- *Uni, apprenticeship, or straight to work?* — The financial reality of each path; student loans explained honestly; earnings potential comparison
- *How do people actually make money?* — Income sources beyond employment; realistic expectations; distinguishing sustainable income from get-rich-quick

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"Which path pays off?"** — Players compare three post-18 routes (university, apprenticeship, straight to work) for a character with a specific goal. Using real earnings data, they answer multiple-choice questions about income at 21, 25, and 30. No prescribed "right answer" — the mechanic reveals trade-offs rather than recommendations, reflecting the genuine complexity of the decision.
- **"Benefits bingo"** — A scenario-based eligibility challenge: given a character's living situation (e.g. working part-time, renting a room, 18 years old), players identify which state benefits apply and how to claim them. True/false and multiple-choice format, building awareness without requiring immediate action.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- Build a milestone notification series tied to the user's age — "You turn 18 in 3 months: here's the ISA, benefits check, and savings account to set up before then" — turning life transitions into specific, timely financial prompts.
- Link directly to Turn2Us or EntitledTo for benefits eligibility from inside the benefits lesson — users complete a quick eligibility check as part of the lesson flow, not as a separate task they'll never do.

## Young Adults (20–25)

As established in [[6. Segment Verdicts|§6]], Young Adults are deprioritised as a primary audience. What this segment needs is not financial education but personalised financial guidance — a structural gap the current product cannot close. Their usage pattern is "pull when needed," not daily habit, and the gamified format is a credibility barrier for many.

However, some content overlap with V2 Older Teens remains valuable — particularly for 19–21-year-olds whose situations closely resemble the older end of the OT cohort.

### Where YA Content Adds Value

The priority JTBDs for Young Adults — manage money wisely ●●●, stop avoiding money ●●●, feel confident ●●●, protect from traps ●●●, deal with adult responsibilities ●●● — are well-documented in [[5. **Suggested lessons example framing**

- *Compound interest: why starting early actually makes a difference* — Real numbers; visual comparison; 5–10 year time horizons, not 40
- *Your credit card is not free money — here's the proof* — APR; minimum payments; the Mohammad scenario; autopay settings
- *How to budget when your income isn't the same every month* — Framework for variable income; floor-and-flex method; real scenarios
- *The 0% trap: how promotional rates catch people out* — Timeline mechanics; what "0% for 12 months" actually means

**Building Skills and Habits**

*Skills — V1 (scenario and choice-based practice):*
- **"What does your money actually do?"** — An income-allocation scenario using realistic adult income figures (e.g. Grace's student finance + bar earnings mix). Players apply a budgeting framework across three months of variable income, identify the shortfall month, and choose the right corrective action. Treats participants as capable adults managing real complexity.
- **"The minimum payment trap"** — The Mohammad scenario in multiple-choice form: given a balance, APR, and minimum payment figure, players predict the total cost over 12 months, then compare it to clearing the full balance monthly. Real numbers, no simplification — the gap between the two answers does the teaching.

*Habit — V2 ideas:*
- End the budgeting lesson with a "set it up now" prompt that deep-links into Monzo Spaces or Starling's saving spaces — the lesson completes by triggering a real action, not by returning the user to a home screen.
- Add a one-tap "annual money review" calendar invite at lesson end — a yearly reminder to check and update automation settings — so the system doesn't drift quietly out of date.

**What to exclude at YA level:** Basic concepts that feel patronising (simple needs vs. wants, "what is a bank account"). These can appear as revision references but should not be primary content. YA participants were explicit that oversimplification undermines trust. Scenarios should use realistic income levels based on age-specific national averages.

> **Structural note:** Fully addressing the 20–25 segment would require AI-powered personalisation, open banking integration, and a pull-based reference model rather than a habit-formation push model. The app's strongest launch case is where the gap between capability and need is narrowest — 14–16 for V1 and 17–19 for V2.

## Framing Topics in a Relevant Way

Topic selection determines relevance. Framing determines whether users feel it. Three principles emerged consistently across segments:

**1. Anchor every lesson to a situation the user is already in — or will be in very soon.** Abstract concepts fail; concrete scenarios land. Young Teens respond to small amounts and near-term goals. Older Teens want preparation framing ("should you be investing at your age?"), not theory. Young Adults need content that reflects real conditions — irregular income, multiple income streams — not ideal-world assumptions.

**2. Match the emotional weight of the framing to the stakes.** Playful works for Young Teens, where financial stakes are genuinely small. Older Teens are at the edge of that tolerance — they want to be taken seriously. Young Adults are explicit: the right tone is a capable, direct peer, not a cheerful mascot.

**3. Use the language they already use — not the language of financial education.** Young Teens understand "making money last" but not "budgeting." Older Teens use Revolut, savings pots, and cashback naturally — explaining these is an engagement killer. Young Adults want precision: *"you know interest exists — here's how to actually use it"* respects existing knowledge while filling the gap.
