# 10. First Impressions and Visual Comprehension

## Immediate Impressions & Expectations

- The landing screen communicates purpose and tone before a single word is read — the level-map layout, monster characters, and unlockable progression do the work instantly
- The Duolingo mental model transfers positively: associations of accessible, habitual, low-stakes learning — a strong fit for financial education
- The design sets accurate expectations and delivers on them; expectation alignment at entry is a prerequisite for a satisfying first session
- The single complication: visual language that works for a 13-year-old signals a mismatch to a 19-year-old — an inherent tension in designing a single aesthetic for an audience spanning six developmental years

> *"It's kind of like Duolingo, but like for money."* (Norah, 13, UK)
> *"It kind of looks like games I've got on my phone... it's like you have to complete a level to unlock another one."* (Hugo, 13, UK)
> *"looks like a game but made to make learning about money easy"* (Enoch, 18, UK)

## Visual Comprehension

- The aesthetic is calibrated for Young Teens: monster characters, colour palette, game-style iconography, and Duolingo-like layout all signal playful, accessible, safe
- **The aesthetic age-gap challenge:** A strength for the 14–16 bullseye, it becomes a progressively greater barrier for older users. CZ data strongly validates this — CZ participants across all segments were more likely than UK counterparts to flag the monsters as undermining credibility, confirming this is a cross-market design priority

> *"The monsters, that sounds quite cool."* (Hugo, 13, UK)
> *"Too childish. (...) It seems like it's for schoolchildren."* (Daniel, 24, CZ)
> *"The first impression is that... it's more like a game for kids. But if I then looked at it more, I see some first lessons there, so it could be something educational. It reminds me a bit of Duolingo."* (Jan, 19, CZ)

### YTs (14–16): Where the Design Lands — With Caveats

- The aesthetic lowers the perceived barrier of a financial topic before a single question appears
- The visual language — level map, locked/unlocked states, monster characters — is immediately decoded; participants understood what kind of experience they were entering without being told

> *"It's quite good. It's like not super eye-catching, though, because the colours are quite plain almost. It makes you want to like keep going."* (Olivia, 14, UK)
> *"I like how there is the collection of those monsters. I also like how it is actually graphically designed, that there is some figure that guides the person through it."* (Marie, 14, CZ)

- The "too childish" reaction is not exclusively a CZ finding — Zain (13, UK) also found the monsters better suited to under-9s. But it is more prevalent in CZ: multiple CZ YTs flagged the design as targeting a younger age group, while most UK YTs accepted or welcomed it
- Several CZ participants wanted to customise their guide character (e.g. choosing a human rather than a cartoon mascot) to feel more age-appropriate

### OTs (16–19): Visual Design Creates a Split

- Older Teens decode the visual language clearly — level structure, progression, and app concept land without confusion
- The aesthetic response is not uniform: **UK OTs were split** — Dulcie (16) said "I love it" and read the monster as a guide/companion; Levi (17) found it "fun and exciting." But Aiden (15) perceived it as designed for primary school children; Ovie (15) said it "reminds me of primary school in year seven"
- **CZ OTs similarly split** — Niky, Jan, and Lukáš responded positively; Vilma (15) estimated it suited children up to around 12
- The divide appears driven by individual sensibility around maturity signalling more than age alone — those who accepted it read the monsters as companions; those who rejected it felt treated as younger children

> *"I love it... If I had this app, I would actually want to learn so much about banking."* (Dulcie, 16, UK)
> *"You can see that it is probably made for children. Mainly this design seems to me that it does not completely attract adult people."* (Honza, 16, CZ)

### YAs: Limited to Niche Appeal

- For most Young Adults, the playful design signalled young/easy content — at odds with their expectation that money is a serious topic
- The segment is not monolithic: Enoch (18, UK) was broadly positive; Mia (25, UK) loved the collection system, citing Plum (4M+ users) as evidence that character-driven design can work for older users when underlying utility is clear

> *"Money is an important topic. It should be more serious."* (Althea, 19, UK)

## The Monster Characters: Visual Execution

- Central to brand identity — the app is literally called Money Monsters — but their visual execution is the primary driver of the aesthetic age-gap
- The cartoon style, bright colours, and toy-like design work for the 14–16 core cohort but read as "childish" to progressively older users
- Even within the YT segment, CZ moderation flagged that several participants rejected the aesthetic — not wanting to be treated like young children. This makes adaptation more urgent than the age-gap alone suggests
- Two distinct issues: (1) aesthetic style feels too young; (2) the learner's relationship to the monsters is visually ambiguous — unclear whether you play *as* or *collect* them
- **Key cross-market signal:** Older users in both UK and CZ responded more positively to Mr. Korunka/Mr. Money (the human guide) than to the monsters. Jan (19, CZ), Bara (18, CZ), Natalie (24, CZ), and Anna (21, CZ) all described him as more "trustworthy" or "credible." The monsters trigger the age-mismatch reaction; the human guide does not

> *"When I see it, I look and see that it's more for kids... So if it wasn't aimed directly at children, you could make different characters than, for example monsters."* (Veronika, 13, CZ)
> *"Well, my first impression is that it looks a lot like Duolingo. And maybe too childish for me... those monsters seem so childish to me, more for children..."* (Ladislav, 13, CZ)

## Structural Visual Conventions: What Works

- Progress, status, and locked/unlocked states were understood and welcomed across all segments
- The level-map layout, green vs gray distinction, and implied progressive difficulty were universally decoded — no explanation needed
- The UX architecture is sound — the app doesn't need to change how it works for older users, it needs visuals that look less childish
- A shared architecture with age-differentiated visual skins is the right direction; not a fundamental redesign of the learning model

> *"So this application, as it is in the form of a game, I think it's good. It's not an article. It's not some text. It's in the form of those levels? I think that's good."* (Honza, 16, CZ)
> *"I think it's good... that you just learn something at the same time, and when you learn something, then you can unlock something and get more things."* (Veronika, 13, CZ)
