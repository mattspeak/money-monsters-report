# 12. Prioritised Recommendations

The recommendations below are split into two tiers: what needs to be done before or at launch (v1), and what should be queued for the next development cycle (v2+). The v1 list is not exhaustive — it identifies the minimum changes with the highest impact on launch success, specifically for the Young Teen primary audience. The v2+ list reflects structural investments that are clearly necessary but require more development time than a launch window permits.

The filter applied to v1: would not doing this meaningfully damage the first session experience for a Young Teen user? If yes, it belongs in v1. Everything else is v2+.

---

## 12.1 v1 of Money Monsters

### 12.1.1 Land the launch learning content and mechanics

The scenario-based, dialogue-driven lesson format is the product's strongest asset — participants across all segments responded positively to it, and it should be the core creative vehicle throughout. The task for v1 is to make sure the format is executed consistently and the known friction points are resolved before launch.

**Scenario specificity by segment.** Characters, amounts, and situations must reflect the actual financial lives of the target user. A question about a £500 windfall lands differently for a 13-year-old with pocket money than a 23-year-old with a salary. For the Young Teen launch audience, scenarios should be anchored in realistic pocket-money amounts, social spending situations, and near-term saving goals — not financial products they haven't encountered. Where the same topic appears across segments, the scenario changes even if the concept doesn't.

**Key information must appear at the point of decision.** Mohammad (23) identified the most important UX principle in the lesson design: *"where content is split over a couple of screens, it's really important that the key detail is on the second screen"* — the screen where the question is asked. Participants should not need to remember details from a previous screen to answer a question on the current one. Where a lesson introduces information and then tests it, the essential reference must remain accessible.

**Clarify character identity from the first interaction.** Aiden (15) described confusion about whether he was playing *as* the monster or *collecting* it — a design problem that broke the scenario logic for him. The player's relationship to in-app characters must be established clearly and maintained consistently: are they inhabiting the character or advising one? The learner should always know. This is a one-time orientation problem, fixable with clear framing at character introduction.

**Calibrate onboarding as calibration, not a test.** The quiz framing should make its purpose explicit from the first screen: the app is learning about the user's situation to select relevant content, not assessing competence. Scenario-based and behavioural questions consistently outperformed definitional tests across all segments — the quiz should ask how participants behave with money, not what they know. Questions should draw from segment-calibrated scenarios: amounts, products, and situations that are realistic for the target audience's actual financial world.

**Explanations after every answer, right or wrong.** The try-again mechanic is well received. Post-answer explanation adds further value in both directions — Mia (25) explicitly valued being told *why* she was right as much as being told why she was wrong. Every answer interaction should end with understanding, not just a result. Explanations should be brief, plain-language, and conversational.

**Avoid maths-heavy questions for Young Teens.** Aiden (15) identified calculation-based questions as a significant switch-off: *"I don't like maths."* The goal is financial understanding, not arithmetic competence. Where numerical reasoning is necessary, build it into the scenario framing rather than requiring the learner to calculate. For v1, prefer question formats that ask participants to reason and choose over questions that require them to compute.

### 12.1.2 Structure the topics

The progression map and topic structure communicate the app's content architecture — and they need to do it clearly enough that a new user can orient themselves without instruction.

**Add glossary support for unfamiliar financial terms.** Aiden (15) flagged ISAs and cryptocurrency in the onboarding quiz as terms he didn't know, without a way to understand what he was being asked about. Multiple Young Adult participants needed definitions for financial product terminology mid-lesson. A lightweight tap-for-definition mechanic — already partially present in the lesson overlay functionality participants responded well to — should be extended consistently across onboarding, lessons, and topic maps. The principle: users should never need to leave the app to understand a word in it.

**Make topic names legible to the target audience.** Topic names on the progression map need to communicate content, not just label it. "Forecasting with Frank" communicates very little about what the user will learn; "Borrowing with Cardi" is more suggestive but still abstract. For Young Teens in particular, topic names should give a direct signal about relevance to their life — not every user will self-identify as needing to understand borrowing, but many will recognise that they want to know how to make money last.

**Sequence content to match the target user's financial reality.** The research suggests a clear content priority hierarchy for Young Teen launch: needs vs. wants and impulse control first (the most universal and most pressing concern), then saving toward a specific goal, then simple budgeting rules (50/30/20 landed well and was new information for both Aiden and Levi). More advanced content — compound interest, savings account mechanics, borrowing — works better as second-tier unlocks once foundational habits are established. The level that is shown first sets the expectation for everything that follows; it should be the content the largest share of users will find immediately relevant.

**Show what's locked and why.** The locked/unlocked progression map worked well across all segments — participants consistently described locked content as aspiration rather than frustration. The visual design handles this competently. What's missing is an explicit preview: a brief indication of what a locked lesson covers, so participants have a reason to progress rather than just a prompt to do so. Isaac (12) and Sam (14) both described the forward pull of the map positively — a short content teaser for each locked lesson would strengthen this without requiring structural changes to the progression model.

### 12.1.3 Quick-win gamification mechanics

The current gamification layer is functional but insufficiently deep to sustain engagement beyond the first few sessions. These are targeted additions that materially improve the retention loop without requiring a fundamental rebuild.

**Give monsters a second layer of consequence.** Collection without consequence is motivation that exhausts itself quickly — Althea (19) named the problem precisely, and Zain (20) described expecting monsters to *"unlock app features, avatar customisations, or leaderboard abilities."* For v1, even a minimal second layer changes the proposition: a collected monster that unlocks a bonus lesson, a cosmetic skin option, or a featured placement on a hypothetical future leaderboard is meaningfully more compelling than a monster that simply appears in a grid. The specific mechanic matters less than the principle: collecting should lead somewhere.

**Add XPs alongside streaks.** The streak mechanic is understood and appreciated but operates as a binary — you're in a streak or you've broken it. XPs add a cumulative progress layer that survives a missed day: breaking a streak is less catastrophic if a parallel progress indicator continues to accumulate. Enoch (18) explicitly requested an XP system like Duolingo's, with XP based on completion time and accuracy. For v1, XPs don't need to be spendable or exchangeable — simply accumulating and visible is enough to create a second motivation axis alongside the streak.

**Add a progress indicator to the onboarding quiz.** Levi (17) identified this as his single most practical improvement request: a simple completion indicator showing how far through the quiz the user is. It is a minor implementation effort with a disproportionate impact on the experience of onboarding — it reduces abandonment by making the end visible. Currently the quiz reads as open-ended; a progress bar closes that uncertainty.

**Preview the next monster before it's earned.** Sam (14) described the app as making you feel like you're *"working towards something"* — the forward pull. The progression map does some of this work, but a visible preview of the next collectible monster (name, appearance, theme) at the end of each completed lesson would strengthen the anticipatory engagement that makes collection genuinely motivating. This is a display change, not a system change.

## 12.2 Queue for Version 2+

These recommendations are clearly evidenced by the research and will meaningfully improve the product — but they require development investment that exceeds the launch window. Each is a genuine product priority, not a nice-to-have.

### 12.2.1 Topic selection and non-linear learning paths

The linear progression model is well-suited to Young Teens and works as a launch architecture. It becomes a structural barrier for Older Teens and Young Adults, who arrive with specific questions and resist being locked into prerequisite content. Zain (20) resisted the linear model entirely; Grace (19) described wanting topic-based access *"when facing specific financial decisions or problems"*; Althea (19) would use the app situationally, not sequentially.

A situational entry point — a topic index, searchable glossary, or *"what do you need today?"* routing question — would serve pull-based users without dismantling the streak and progression architecture that serves habit-based ones. The two models are not mutually exclusive; they require different front-door design. For v2, the task is adding a non-linear access path as a parallel route, not replacing the progression map.

### 12.2.2 More interactive question formats (beyond multiple choice)

Multiple choice is the dominant format, and it was broadly accepted. The limitation is that it asks users to pattern-match rather than reason: when the wrong answers are too easily dismissed, the question has no learning value. Participants noted this at both ends of the age range.

v2 should establish which input modalities are technically feasible and design the question mix to use them: drag-and-drop allocation (assign spending to needs/wants/savings), slider-based decisions (how much would you save this month?), simulated product interfaces (the bank login simulation was a well-received moment in the lesson), and tap-to-reveal mechanics. Varied modalities sustain attention and better reflect how financial decisions are actually made — they are not aesthetic improvements, they are learning design improvements.

### 12.2.3 Bolder gamification: spend and earn XPs, monster depth

The v1 additions establish XPs and give monsters a second layer. v2 is where those mechanics mature into a system.

Isaac (12) laid out the design brief clearly: spendable points, monster trading and swapping, and map-based mini-games where financial decisions have navigable consequences. Enoch (18) wanted XP with jeopardy — losing XP for wrong answers, earning more for speed and accuracy. Zain (20) expected monsters to unlock features and avatar customisations. The direction across all segments is consistent: consequence and depth. Collecting without spending, earning without risking, progressing without competing — these are thin versions of the mechanics that make games genuinely engaging.

v2 gamification should introduce: a spend layer for XPs (redeeming against cosmetics, content unlocks, or a competitive feature), consequence mechanics in monster collection (the second layer that makes collecting matter), and the foundation architecture for the social features that follow.

### 12.2.4 Social features: leaderboards, peer comparison, sharing monsters

The social layer is effectively absent, and its absence is mentioned unprompted by participants across all three segments. Levi (17) made the dependency explicit: *"if other people use it, I would definitely start using it"* — social proof is not a feature preference but a description of the adoption mechanism. Without peer engagement, retention relies entirely on individual motivation.

The social feature roadmap should develop in sequence: competitive streak comparison with friends first (lowest friction, highest relevance to existing mechanics), then leaderboards within friend groups or cohorts, then public leaderboards for broader competitive engagement (Zain's preference). Sharing monsters — Ovie's suggestion of a competitive element to collect more than friends — is a natural social extension of a mechanic that already exists. The social layer doesn't require new core content; it requires a visibility and comparison layer on top of existing progress data.

The caveat from premium direction applies here: basic social features should be free, as they are primarily adoption and retention drivers. Competitive depth can be a premium differentiator.

### 12.2.5 Age-differentiated visual themes or separate skins by segment

The core structural finding from the visual comprehension work is that the UX architecture is sound across all segments and the aesthetic is the problem. Older Teens and Young Adults responded positively to the level map, progression logic, streak mechanic, and collection concept — and negatively to the monster characters, cartoon visual style, and primary colour palette.

The fix is an age-up that preserves the motivational architecture while replacing the surface through which it's expressed. Ovie (15): *"replace monsters with badges or more age-appropriate rewards — maintain competitive collection element but with mature visual design."* Zain (20): *"money-themed tokens [coins] as a more credible equivalent."* The principle is decoupling the playful identity from the structural gamification: the latter can survive a design evolution that dials back the former.

For v2, the task is developing a visual skin for the 16–18 segment — more minimal, less toy-like, preserving the progression and collection architecture under a different aesthetic register. A full Young Adult skin is a longer-term investment that would require a more significant redesign commitment. The 16–18 skin is feasible as a v2 target and would materially expand the addressable audience.

### 12.2.6 Situational routing via onboarding

The current onboarding quiz assigns users to content without enough depth to genuinely tailor the experience — Zain (20) noted that he didn't feel the quiz results significantly affected subsequent lesson content. The onboarding quiz's promise is personalisation; v2 should make the personalisation real.

Situational routing means building different content pools or lesson sequences triggered by user profile signals: a student sees content about overdrafts, student loan management, and term-time budgeting; a user who has just started their first job sees content about payslips, tax codes, and PAYE basics; a user preparing to move out sees content about renting, utility bills, and the costs of independence. The quiz already asks behavioural questions — the work is connecting those answers to genuinely differentiated content rather than a generic lesson sequence.

This is the most significant content investment in the v2+ list, but it is also the recommendation that most directly addresses the structural gap identified in section 10: the habit-based daily model versus the situational pull-based usage pattern of older users. A product that can meet users where they are — at the specific financial moment they're navigating — is a substantially more useful product, and a substantially harder one to stop using.
