# 6. App Concept: First Impressions of stimuli and Visual Comprehension

## 6.1 Immediate Impressions & Expectations

The Money Monsters landing screen does something few finance apps achieve: it communicates its purpose and tone instantly. The level-map layout, the monster characters, and the unlockable progression structure combine to signal a gamified learning experience before a single word is read. Participants across all segments understood what they were looking at within seconds — and almost all reached for the same reference point: Duolingo.

This immediate recognition is a meaningful asset. Duolingo carries strong associations with accessible, habitual, low-stakes learning — a mental model well-suited to financial education. Lottie (13) made the comparison positively and unprompted. Norah (13) echoed it. Mia (25) recognised the format immediately and found it reassuring. Enoch (18) articulated the concept precisely: *"looks like a game but made to make learning about money easy."* The concept lands clearly.

The expectations set by the landing screen were also broadly accurate: participants expected progressive content unlock, some kind of streak or engagement mechanic, and monsters as a progress reward. These expectations were met by the research stimulus — expectation alignment at entry is a prerequisite for a satisfying first session, and this design achieves it.

The complication arrives when the question of *who this is for* enters the frame. The same visual language that makes the app immediately legible to a 13-year-old signals a mismatch to a 19-year-old. This is not a flaw in the design concept — it is a consequence of building a single aesthetic for an audience that spans six developmental years.

## 6.2 The Aesthetic Age-Gap Challenge

The app's current visual aesthetic is optimised for Young Teens. The monster characters, the colour palette, the game-style iconography, and the Duolingo-like layout all point to the same design sensibility: playful, accessible, safe. For the 12–14 age band this is a strength. For older users it becomes a progressively greater barrier — one that increases in severity with age and with financial seriousness.

### 6.2.1 Where the current design works

For Young Teens, the aesthetic functions as intended. Monster characters are clearly understood and welcomed. The level-map progression is immediately engaging. The gamified framing lowers the perceived difficulty of financial content — it signals "this will be manageable" before the first question appears. Hugo (13) found the monster name catchy and the design cool. Sam (14) described the app as "easy to use" and not intimidating. Isaac (12) noted that "monsters make it more interesting for young people than a plain interface." For this segment, the visual design does exactly what it should: it removes the barrier to entry for a topic that might otherwise feel adult, serious, and off-putting.

The structural conventions also work well across the full age range. The level map, the locked/unlocked progression logic, the streak concept, and the collection mechanic were understood and positively received even by participants who disliked the aesthetic. Enoch (18) and Zain (20, YA) appreciated the unlockable chunk structure despite finding the overall design "a bit young." The implication: the UX architecture beneath the visual layer is sound across all segments. The problem is not the model — it is how the model is dressed.

### 6.2.2 Where it starts to fail

For Older Teens, the design creates friction. Ovie (15) described the landing screen as feeling like "primary school in year seven." Aiden (15) described it as "immediately perceived as designed for primary school children." Levi (17) was more positive initially — the gamified structure engaged him — but later flagged that the monster characters would need to feel less toy-like for a 17-year-old audience to take the app seriously.

The core issue for this segment is not that they dislike gamification — they don't. Aiden, Ovie, and Levi all responded positively to streak mechanics, progression, and collection-based rewards. The issue is that the specific visual execution (monster characters, primary-palette colours, cartoon guides) reads as targeted at a younger audience. For a 15-17 year old beginning to form a financial identity and see themselves as a developing adult, the aesthetic implicitly undermines the credibility of the content before it has been seen.

### 6.2.3 Where it actively damages credibility

For a significant portion of Young Adults, the design actively works against engagement. Grace (19) described the landing screen as "childish" and "too playful," stating that the visual design "prevents serious engagement with financial content." Mohammad (23) went further: *"feels like you're just taking them, putting them into a child seat"* — the aesthetic communicating an assumption that users can't be trusted with serious content. Althea (19) identified the tension precisely: *"there should be a degree of seriousness — it's an important topic."* For these participants, playful design signals low stakes; financial education requires the opposite signal.

It is important to note that not all Young Adults rejected the design. Enoch (18) found it appealing and appropriate. Mia (25) responded warmly to the "simple, cute aesthetic." The credibility ceiling is most pronounced among participants who are further along toward financial independence and who bring a more serious, functional relationship with money to the experience. But for product purposes, the most sceptical users define the ceiling — and for Young Adults, that scepticism is meaningful enough to require a design response.

## 6.3 The Monster Characters

The monsters are the most debated element of the visual design and the point where the age-gap challenge is most acute. Their role is threefold: they are the collection mechanic (progress reward), the narrative vehicle in lessons (bringing financial scenarios to life), and the app's brand identity — the name is literally Money Monsters.

For Young Teens, the monsters work well. The collection concept is understood and motivating. Lottie (13) responded to the competitive collecting dynamic. Isaac (12) found monsters made the app feel more interesting than a plain interface. The association with collecting, competing, and unlocking aligns with the broader gaming sensibility of the 12–14 age group.

For Older Teens and most Young Adults, the monster aesthetic is the single most cited reason for the design feeling "childish." The principle this suggests is not that monsters should be removed — the collection mechanic and the progression logic they embody are sound — but that their visual execution needs to mature alongside the audience. Ovie (15) suggested badges as an alternative reward format for older users. Zain (20, YA) suggested money-themed tokens (coins) as a more credible equivalent. Aiden (15) preferred that the primary monster character be introduced and named earlier, creating attachment rather than confusion. The collection mechanic is wanted; the specific character design needs to be segment-sensitive.

One specific design problem emerged from Aiden's session: confusion about whether he was playing *as* the monster character or *collecting* it. The principle extracted — independent of this specific design — is that the player/learner's relationship to in-app characters must be clearly established and consistently maintained. Ambiguity about identity within the learning experience breaks the scenario logic and reduces engagement.

## 6.4 Structural Visual Conventions: What Works

Beneath the character design debate, the structural visual conventions of the app commanded broad, cross-segment approval. The level-map layout — a vertically arranged sequence of lessons with locked/unlocked states — was understood by every participant and described positively by almost all. The visual distinction between current, available, and locked content was clear. The implied promise of progressive difficulty and reward for completion was motivating across all three segments.

Olivia (14) recognised game-like level progression as "making you want to keep going." Enoch (18) understood the progression logic immediately — *"green = next, gray = locked"* — and found the sequential completion structure reassuring rather than restrictive. Zain (20, YA) liked the bite-sized, unlockable chunk format even while finding the aesthetic too young.

The implication is significant: the app does not need to change its structural UX to serve older users. It needs a visual layer that can express the same structural logic in an aesthetic appropriate to each segment. A shared architecture with age-differentiated visual skins is the right direction — not a fundamental redesign of the learning model.

## 6.5 The Core Design Tension: One App, Multiple Audiences

The central finding from the visual comprehension work is that the app's current aesthetic is optimised for one segment and creates increasing friction for the other two. This is not a criticism of the current design — it is an inherent challenge in building a single product for users who span from 12 to 25.

The design choices that lower the barrier to entry for a 13-year-old are precisely the choices that raise the credibility barrier for a 23-year-old. There is no single aesthetic that satisfies both ends of the spectrum simultaneously. The practical implication for launch: **Money Monsters needs to be clear about which segment it is primarily designing for.**

If Young Teens are the launch bullseye — which the research supports as the strongest product-market fit — the current aesthetic direction is broadly right. Incremental maturation (less toy-like character design, slightly more sophisticated colour palette, reduced cartoon-heavy iconography) will address the edges of the Older Teen segment without alienating Young Teens. If the product aspires to serve Young Adults credibly, a meaningfully different visual presentation is required — not just tweaks, but a different aesthetic register. The same content, different skin; the structural model is sound enough to support it.
