# 6. App Concept: First Impressions of stimuli and Visual Comprehension

## 6.1 Immediate Impressions & Expectations

- The landing screen communicates purpose and tone before a single word is read — the level-map layout, monster characters, and unlockable progression do the work instantly
- The Duolingo mental model transfers positively: it carries associations of accessible, habitual, low-stakes learning — a strong fit for financial education
- The design sets accurate expectations (progressive unlock, streaks, monsters as reward) and delivers on them; expectation alignment at entry is a prerequisite for a satisfying first session, and this achieves it
- The single complication: the visual language that makes the app immediately legible to a 13-year-old signals a mismatch to a 19-year-old — an inherent tension in designing a single aesthetic for an audience spanning six developmental years

**Participant perspectives**
- Participants across all segments understood what they were looking at within seconds; almost all reached for Duolingo as their reference point, positively and unprompted
- Enoch (18): *"looks like a game but made to make learning about money easy"*

## 6.2 The Aesthetic Age-Gap Challenge

- The app's current visual aesthetic is calibrated for Young Teens: monster characters, colour palette, game-style iconography, and the Duolingo-like layout all point to the same sensibility — playful, accessible, safe
- This calibration is a deliberate strength for 12–14 year olds; for older users it becomes a progressively greater barrier, increasing in severity with age and financial seriousness

### 6.2.1 Where the current design works

- For Young Teens, the aesthetic does exactly what it should: remove the perceived difficulty of financial content before the first question appears
- The structural conventions — level map, locked/unlocked progression, streak concept, collection mechanic — work across the full age range regardless of aesthetic preferences
- The UX architecture beneath the visual layer is sound; the problem is not the model, it is how the model is dressed

**Participant perspectives**
- Hugo (13): found the design cool; Sam (14): described it as easy to use and not intimidating; Isaac (12): "monsters make it more interesting for young people than a plain interface"
- Enoch (18) and Zain (20): appreciated the unlockable chunk structure and progression logic despite finding the overall aesthetic "a bit young"

### 6.2.2 Where it starts to fail

- Older Teens do not reject gamification — they respond well to streaks, progression, and collection rewards
- The friction is the specific visual execution: monster characters, primary-palette colours, and cartoon guides read as targeted at a younger audience
- For 15–17 year olds beginning to form a financial identity, the aesthetic undermines the credibility of the content before it has been seen

**Participant perspectives**
- Ovie (15): the landing screen feels like "primary school in year seven"; Aiden (15): "immediately perceived as designed for primary school children"
- Levi (17): initially engaged by the gamified structure, but later flagged that monster characters would need to feel less toy-like for a 17-year-old to take the app seriously
- All three responded positively to streak mechanics and progression — the model engages them; the visual register does not

### 6.2.3 Where it actively damages credibility

- For a significant portion of Young Adults, playful design signals low stakes — the precise opposite of what financial education requires
- The credibility ceiling is most pronounced among participants further along toward financial independence, with a more functional, serious relationship with money
- The response is not universal, but for product purposes the most sceptical users define the ceiling — and that scepticism is meaningful enough to require a design response

**Participant perspectives**
- Grace (19): described the landing screen as "childish" and "too playful"; the visual design "prevents serious engagement with financial content"
- Mohammad (23): *"feels like you're just taking them, putting them into a child seat"*
- Althea (19): *"there should be a degree of seriousness — it's an important topic"*
- Enoch (18) and Mia (25): found the aesthetic appealing and appropriate — rejection is not universal, but the pattern is directionally clear

## 6.3 The Monster Characters

- The monsters carry a triple burden: collection mechanic, narrative vehicle in lessons, and core brand identity — the app is literally called Money Monsters; they cannot simply be removed
- The mechanic itself is sound and wanted across segments; the visual execution is the problem — it needs to mature alongside the audience
- A distinct structural issue sits alongside the aesthetic one: the learner's relationship to in-app characters must be clearly established from the outset; ambiguity about whether you play *as* or *collect* the monster breaks scenario logic and reduces engagement

**Participant perspectives**
- Young Teens: Lottie (13) responded to the competitive collecting dynamic; Isaac (12) found monsters more engaging than a plain interface
- Older Teens and most Young Adults: the monster aesthetic is the single most cited reason the design feels "childish"
- Suggested alternatives: Ovie (15) proposed badges; Zain (20) suggested money-themed tokens or coins; Aiden (15) preferred the primary monster be introduced and named earlier to build attachment rather than confusion
- Aiden: could not determine whether he was playing *as* the monster character or *collecting* it — the ambiguity was disorienting

## 6.4 Structural Visual Conventions: What Works

- The structural visual conventions commanded broad, cross-segment approval — independent of the aesthetic debate
- The level-map layout, the locked/unlocked distinction, and the implied promise of progressive difficulty were universally understood and positively received
- The implication is significant: the app does not need to change its structural UX to serve older users — it needs a visual layer that expresses the same logic in an age-appropriate aesthetic
- A shared architecture with age-differentiated visual skins is the right direction; not a fundamental redesign of the learning model

**Participant perspectives**
- Olivia (14): game-like level progression "makes you want to keep going"
- Enoch (18): understood progression immediately — *"green = next, gray = locked"* — and found the sequential structure reassuring rather than restrictive
- Zain (20): liked the bite-sized, unlockable chunk format even while finding the aesthetic too young

## 6.5 The Core Design Tension: One App, Multiple Audiences

- The design choices that lower the barrier to entry for a 13-year-old are precisely the choices that raise the credibility barrier for a 23-year-old — no single aesthetic satisfies both ends of the spectrum simultaneously
- **The practical implication for launch: Money Monsters needs to be explicit about which segment it is primarily designing for**
- If Young Teens are the launch bullseye — which the research supports as the strongest product-market fit — the current aesthetic direction is broadly right; incremental maturation (less toy-like characters, a slightly more sophisticated palette, reduced cartoon-heavy iconography) will address the edges of the Older Teen segment without alienating Young Teens
- If the product aspires to serve Young Adults credibly, a meaningfully different visual register is required — not tweaks, but a different aesthetic language applied to the same underlying structure; the model is sound enough to carry it
