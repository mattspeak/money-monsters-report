# 5. What Do Young People Actually Want from Financial Education?

## Jobs to Be Done

***What are Jobs to Be Done?** 'JTBD' helps us understand why people engage with a product; not just what they do, but what they're trying to achieve. The key idea is that people don't buy products, they "hire" them to get a job done.*

For Money Monsters, this means asking not "what does this user need to learn?" but "what is this user trying to accomplish in their financial life right now, and how can the app help them do it?"

The answers differ meaningfully by segment — which has direct implications for content selection, lesson framing, and how the app positions itself to each audience.

### Overview

The research asked participants to rank a number of statements (summarized below across don't care, kind of important, and very important). Below are the outcomes participants considered 'very important').

### What This Means for Money Monsters

- **Young Teens** want practical, near-term help — managing money wisely and saving toward something concrete
- **Older Teens** have shifted to real income and real risk, with smart money management and protection from manipulation the dominant priorities cross-market
- **Young Adults** are primarily motivated by anxiety and avoidance rather than knowledge gaps, making emotional control and habit change the core desired outcome versus financial education in the most literal sense.

## Young Teens (13–15) — Jobs to Be Done

Young Teens are at an early stage of financial awareness. Most have limited, irregular income (pocket money, birthday cash, chores) and minimal exposure to formal financial products. Their financial world is small but emotionally significant — and what they want from financial education reflects that reality.

### Manage my money wisely / make it last ●●●

- The core tension is impulse control in social situations — spending with friends, the canteen, small habitual purchases — rather than abstract budgeting. 
- **For MM:** Content should be grounded in realistic everyday scenarios: small amounts, short time horizons, temptation in social contexts — with tools like needs vs. wants, spending triggers, and simple mental budgeting rules.

> Lottie (13, UK) describes herself as *"very impulse driven, our whole family are spenders."* She gets told to save but admits she never follows through.
> Zain (13, UK) spent £11 at a shop *"without thinking"* while out with friends. He explicitly flags spending with friends as his biggest money challenge — he can save toward a goal at home, but loses control the moment he's out with others.

### Save toward a meaningful goal ●●●

- Every participant had a recent example of goal-directed saving — a gaming console, a PC, trainers — all concrete, personal, and near-term, not abstract future plans.
- **For MM** the risk is that abstract lessons about long-term financial futures land poorly here. What works is connecting concepts to something the participant is already motivated by. Saving mechanics, simple compound growth over months, and goal-tracking features all serve this job directly.

> *"Right now I save for new equipment for refereeing and then I want to save for a new mobile phone."* — Ladislav (13, CZ)
> *"I would like to go to Korea sometime, so I would save for a plane ticket."* — Jaroslav (14, CZ)

### Feel confident, not stressed/embarrassed about money ●●○

- Young Teens the emotional territory is more immediate and more social: not wanting to look stupid in conversations.
- Contextual: it surfaces most strongly for participants who have already been in situations where they felt out of their depth. Those without prior exposure tend to rated lower.
- **For MM:** Confidence-building material needs to respect their self-image while genuinely closing knowledge gaps as many YT participants also describe the app as looking "too childish" or "cringe".

> *"I don't want to feel embarrassed when money is discussed because I don't understand it."* — Franta (13, CZ)
> 
> Ladislav (13, CZ) described a habit of pretending to understand when unsure — a behavioural signal that the embarrassment is already being managed through concealment rather than addressed.
> 
> *"I don't want to feel silly when money comes up in conversations."* — Isaac (12, UK), rated Very Important
> *"I want to feel less worried about money stuff I hear at home."* — Norah (13, UK), rated Very Important

## Older Teens (16–18) — Jobs to Be Done

-Older Teens occupy a transitional zone. They are starting to earn real income (part-time jobs, zero-hours contracts), approaching or already making significant financial decisions (apprenticeships vs. university, first credit products), and are more aware of the financial world — and its risks — than Young Teens. 

### Manage my money wisely / make it last ●●●

- The shift from Young Teens is clear: this is no longer about small/infrequent pocket money. These respondents have part-time earnings, zero-hours contracts, Vinted income, or cash from chores — and the job is to make those real earnings count.
- Czech Older Teens are highly focused on tracking their current real-world income (part-time jobs, pocket money) and planning future expenses. When asked to design their own ideal app, multiple OT participants completely ignored gamification and instead drew tools for tracking spending, analyzing expense history, and setting monthly budgets.
- **For MM:** Learning experiences must go beyond budgeting knowledge and engage with real decision-making scenarios and in future versions adopt budgeting utility.

> Levi (17, UK) earns £12/hour on a zero-hours contract and wants to ensure it goes on useful things.
> Ovie (15, UK) sells vintage clothes on Vinted.
> Aiden (15, UK) has saved for a PC and DJ decks and frames smart choices as avoiding decisions that "destroy" his financial position.

### Protect myself from financial traps and manipulation ●●●

- The highest ranked job for CZ OT (and top 3 for UK participants). These participants are starting to encounter real financial products and real risks for the first time, often without adult supervision.
- **CZ:** Often driven by school IT lessons covering scam awareness. 
- **For MM:** Content should focus on recognising manipulation patterns and developing healthy scepticism — not a checklist of things to watch out for.

>Ovie (15, UK) recognising that scams can be subtle and not always obvious.
> In CZ, Honza's teacher was scammed; Jan's grandmother works in insurance and emphasised financial security
> Enoch (18, UK) lost £100 to a sophisticated Telegram-based job scam requiring staged deposits.

### Feel confident and not stressed or embarrassed about money ●●●

- Beneath the surface, this reflects the coming transition: all OT participants are within 1–3 years of significant financial independence. The horizon is visible and slightly daunting.
- **For MM:** Content should focus on building readiness — what's coming, what you'll need to understand, and how to lay the foundations now.

> Levi (17, UK) described his relationship with money as "on edge," particularly in conversations with financially knowledgeable people.
>  In CZ, this was widely rated Very Important, connected to approaching financial independence.

### Save toward a meaningful goal ●●○

- Meaningful goals have evolved with new sources of income and aspirations. They are actively saving for significant milestones (e.g cars, trips).
- OTs rated the ability to manage money more important than saving for meaningful goals.
- **For MM:** Saving mechanics should reflect OT's evolved goal horizons — longer-term, higher-value targets tied to identity and experience (cars, travel, independence). Frame saving as the smart money manager's move rather than the primary financial job — a supporting behaviour, not the headline.

> Honza (16, CZ), saving for a car, noticed that *”If we don't save anything, we won't have anything to work with. That's why I put it above the money management”*.
> Niky (15, CZ), underlined his strong existing self-discipline: *”I don't really care about that to be honest, because I know I won't give up and when I want something, I will save up for it”*.

### Prepare for living independently ●●○

- Whilst only 1/12 respondents rated 'Very Important', 10/12 rated this 'kinda important'. This suggests that independence is not yet a current reality for most OT participants, but the horizon is close enough to register.
- The job is less about urgency and more about quiet anticipation — knowing that things like rent, bills, and bank accounts are coming and wanting some preparation.
- **For MM:** An “what's coming” content layer — short, forward-looking explainers on rent, bills, jobs — can build quiet readiness without feeling premature or alarmist. Position this as preparation for independence rather than urgency, which matches OT's own framing of the concern.

## Young Adults (18–25) — Jobs to Be Done

Young Adults are the segment for whom financial education is most immediately urgent — and yet also the segment the current app format is structurally least equipped to serve. Their financial situations are real and extremely varied: rent, student loans, credit cards, mortgages, irregular income, pensions. 

Their number of 'very important' ranked statements per respondent reflect the pressure and complexity of that reality. 

### Deal with real adult responsibilities (rent, bills, credit, debt) ●●● 

- For some this is a current reality; for others it is imminent. This is not about financial anxiety in the abstract, but about the specific mechanics of adult financial life.
- **For MM:** the app's current content framing does not fully address this — lessons about saving concepts and vocabulary sit upstream of what YA users actually need. This is the structural gap between financial literacy and financial guidance. 

> Mohammad (23, UK): "That's just basic how life goes — you need to get sorted first before you think about anything else."
> Bara (CZ) is managing rent and daily costs independently.
> Daniel (CZ) has a rent commitment and a business loan.
> Natalie (24, CZ) is on parental leave navigating allowance calculations and a mortgage.
> Anna (21, CZ) still lives at home but anticipates needing this knowledge within the year.


### Manage my money wisely / make it last ●●●

- Both UK / CZ rated it highly and highlighted real issues like mortgages, parental leave allowances as well as learning about more formal concepts like AER/RPSN and the credit register.
- The emotional dimension is as important as the practical one: this is not a knowledge gap alone — it is an anxiety and avoidance pattern that financial literacy content can partially address.
- **For MM:** Lessons should address both the practical and emotional layer — framing content around anxiety relief and day-to-day control rather than abstract financial literacy principles. Where possible, connect directly to real adult financial products (credit registers, AER/RPSN, parental allowances) rather than simplified proxies.

> Anna (21, CZ), plans to move out from her parents and rent own apartment. She is aware that “will need to have a budget so that I don't end up at zero. That is important”.
> Mohammad (23, UK) wants to be able to go out without worrying about the dinner bill.
> Natalie (24, CZ) is dealing with allowances in motherhood and notices that “it's very difficult to understand them”, so she would value “an application in which the information is written in human terms”.
### Stop avoiding money situations and start handling ●●● 

- Dominant in the UK but less prevalent in CZ where users prioritorised the following job which is phrased more proactively.
- This suggests UK users are more reactive than proactive in their response to financial situations.
- **For MM:** Short, action-oriented lessons should acknowledge avoidance without judgement, then redirect toward a concrete, manageable action. The goal is to break the avoidance loop, not to deliver comprehensive financial education.

> Althea (19, UK) describes a cycle of overspend → panic → resolve → repeat, driven by *"not knowing where to start, not laziness."*
> Mia (25, UK) has a repeating end-of-month stress cycle.
> Grace (19, UK) ran out of money in her first year at university and wants to avoid a repeat.

### Feel confident and not stressed or embarrassed about money ●●●

- Stronger in CZ, users want confidence that when real situations arise, they won't be lost. Information that simulates a real situation lands as essential; information delivered before the situation arises feels irrelevant. 
- **For MM:** Content should be structured around situations, not principles and  needs to be triggerable at the moment of need, not assumed as ambient learning.

Grace (19, UK) captures the engagement dynamic precisely: *I seek information out when I need it, I don't need it as daily content.... it's difficult to understand when I look up.* This is not apathy — it's selectivity. 

> Daniel (CZ) builds a 6–7 month expense buffer specifically to never be caught out.
> Jan (CZ) is constantly weighing saving vs. investing options.
> Natalie (24, CZ) is navigating parental leave allowances and finds "it's very difficult to understand them" — she would value "an application where information is written in human terms."

### Protect myself from financial traps and manipulation ●●●

- The framing shifts from OT: this is less about scams and more about the hidden costs embedded in mainstream financial products — BNPL defaults, credit card autopay traps, product fee structures, and promotional rates that expire. These are not edge cases; they are the default behaviour of financial products YAs are already using.
- **For MM:** Content is most valuable when users can search/select relevant content.

> Mohammad (23, UK) was charged interest on a credit card despite having funds to cover the balance — the autopay was set to minimum, not full payment, and he didn't realise until later.
> Bara, Daniel, and Jan (CZ) rate practical protection as Very Important in the context of navigating financial products and services independently.
