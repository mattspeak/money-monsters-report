# 11. Onboarding

## What the Onboarding Experience Involved

The research stimulus included a short quiz-based onboarding sequence designed to assess participants' financial knowledge and attitudes before directing them into personalised lesson content.

The quiz comprised five behavioural and scenario-based questions about money situations and spending habits, guided by Mr. Money — a human character who framed it as a way to understand the participant's starting point, not test their competence.

---

## Recommendations for Onboarding (v1)

**Behaviour-based questions** — Across all segments, participants preferred situational questions over knowledge tests. When offered concept-anchored alternatives, nearly all chose the situational format.

> *"Knowing a definition doesn't mean you have relevant experience or real-world need for it."* (Althea, 19, UK)

**Frame as personalisation, not assessment** — The framing that the app is learning about the user, not testing them, must be explicit from the first screen. Without it, the quiz feels purposeless — and if the personalisation isn't perceptible in what follows, the exercise feels hollow. Zain (20, UK) noted exactly this: *"the quiz answers didn't seem to shape the content at all"*.

**Age-calibrated scenarios, amounts, and terminology** — Questions referencing unfamiliar products or unrealistic amounts alienated participants. A £500 windfall lands differently for a 13-year-old than a 23-year-old. Segment-calibrated question pools mitigate dropouts. Unexplained jargon during onboarding — when trust is still being established — is another parallel risk. 

**Quiz length matched to segment** — Young Teens found 5–7 questions appropriate. Older Teens and Young Adults wanted more depth (7–10 questions); several felt fewer questions would leave the app unable to distinguish their situation. Over-shortening for older segments risks the personalisation feeling superficial.

**Friendly guiding character, not authority** — Reception splits by segment and market:
- **UK YTs:** Positive — Mr. Money's credibility as a human guide was valued. *"It makes it feel like that person who is helping has done this as well."* (Sam, 14, UK)
- **UK OTs:** Split — Dulcie (16) saw him as a "teacher/mentor throughout the process"; others found the mix of childish visual design and human guide tonally inconsistent
- **CZ older users:** Notably positive about Mr. Korunka specifically — Bara, Natalie, and Anna (CZ YAs) described him as more trustworthy and credible than the monsters. Daniel (24, CZ) compared him to a corporate HR trainer — a tone critique, not a rejection
- The character framing matters more than the character's existence. A guide positioned as someone who has navigated money themselves — not an instructor, not a corporate figure — has broad cross-segment appeal

---

## Recommendations for Onboarding (v2+)

*The following reflects the ideal onboarding experience as indicated by this research. It goes beyond current technical constraints and is intended as a direction for the next development cycle.*

**1. Replace the knowledge quiz with a short behavioural screener**
- Frame it as the app learning about the user, not assessing them
- Use situational questions to establish context: do they have a part-time job? a savings account?
- This avoids the "being tested" reaction while still enabling meaningful personalisation

**2. Introduce topic-level onboarding as users progress**
- Embed a brief micro-onboarding at the start of each topic group, not just at app entry
- 2–3 questions before each module establishes existing understanding and places users at the right level within that topic
- Makes personalisation visibly functional — the most common unmet expectation in the research
