# 14. Commercialization Routes

The research explored two commercialization routes: **premiumization of the app** (users paying directly) and **commercial partnerships** with financial/educational institutions (via a question of 'Where would you expect Money Monsters to live?')

---

## Route 1: App Premiumization

**Is the app premium-ready?** Not yet — resistance runs deeper than price sensitivity.

- **Brand risk:** a money app charging subscriptions could itself be the financial trap it warns against — users raised this unprompted
- **Moral tension:** Money Monsters recruits lower-income, lower-literacy households — precisely those least positioned to pay; user-funded sits in direct conflict with charitable purpose
- **Market context:** comparable apps (MoneyTime, NatWest MoneySense, Barclays LifeSkills) are institutionally funded — user-pays is not the established model (see [[2. Market Context]])
- **Financially literate users (OT):** Content flexibility (non-linear navigation, ability to skip known concepts) is a more pressing gap than premium features. Content navigation model may need resolving before premium lands for this sub-group.
- **Young Adults:** Showed most variation — from hard refusal to genuine purchase intent. Strongest conversion signal is personalised, situational support. Freemium is more commercially straightforward than subscription.

*(Monetisation was not explored with YT participants)*
### Premium Potential Explored

| Feature | Signal | Why it works / why it doesn't |
|---|---|---|
| **AI personalised Q&A** | Strong (OT, YA) | Situation-specific financial questions feel worth paying for; explicitly preferred over generic AI |
| **Freemium lives/energy model** | Moderate (OT, YA) | Familiar Duolingo model — but only viable once the core game loop is compelling enough to create demand |
| **Ad removal** | Moderate (OT, YA) | Friction removal, no philosophical conflict with educational mission; low barrier entry point |
| **Social & competitive features** | Longer-term (OT, YA) | Leaderboards, streaks, friend comparisons — needs free social layer first |
| **Tiered content progression** | Conditional (OT) | Acceptable only as progression unlocks, not knowledge purchases |
| **Content paywalls** | Rejected (OT, YA) | Values problem: money education should be free; paywalling knowledge conflicts with charitable purpose |
| **Subscription model** | Rejected (YA) | Structurally ironic — an app about avoiding financial traps charging recurring fees; one-time purchase preferred |
| **Cosmetics / extra levels / topic depth** | Rejected (OT, YA) | Low perceived value; users would research independently; generic AI advice specifically called out |

## Route 2: Commercial Partnerships with Financial Institutions

Multiple participants instinctively placed Money Monsters within a banking context. Ovie (16): *"banks as soon as a teen gets their card... should tell people how to use it."* Multiple participants already use Monzo or Starling — embedded in digital banking ecosystems where integration is plausible.

**The case for this model:**
- Reaches users at a natural trigger point — account opening, first card — rather than asking them to discover the app independently
- Banking endorsement resolves the trust gap the app currently faces with older segments
- Bypasses "paying for education" friction entirely — the bank funds it as customer acquisition or CSR
- Scales without individual marketing cost

**This model is already established:**
- **Lloyds × Doshi** (UK, Feb 2025) — Lloyds co-launched a gamified financial literacy app with Doshi ("the Duolingo of money"), targeting 18–24 year olds on student accounts. Doshi now works with three major UK retail banks (TSB, Santander) and reports 200,000+ active users. Platform deploys as a standalone branded app in under a week, or as embedded web components in an existing bank app — low technical barrier for a bank to pilot.
- **NatWest × Young Enterprise / MoneySense** (UK, ongoing) — School-facing financial education platform, bank-funded as CSR, co-delivered with Young Enterprise. Reaching 2.2 million young people in the UK.
- **WSFS Bank × Greenlight** (US, March 2025) — Bank distributes Greenlight's gamified financial curriculum to customers at account activation. Zero consumer friction, no payment required — the cleanest example of the bank-as-distributor model.

**The gap — and Money Monsters' pitch angle:**
- Doshi targets 18–24. NatWest's MoneySense is school-led and relatively static. Money Monsters' 13–15 core — mobile-first, character-driven, genuinely gamified — sits in a space neither is addressing.
- That's the angle for an early partnership conversation with Monzo or Starling, where user familiarity is already established.

**Recommendation:** Approach digital banks with a 14–17 year old audience pitch — Money Monsters integrated into youth accounts; bank-funded as a customer acquisition tool.
