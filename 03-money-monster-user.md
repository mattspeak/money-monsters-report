# 3. The Money Monster User — Segment Archetypes

These are design targets, not research summaries. Each archetype is a single, specific person — grounded in what the research revealed, but shaped into a concrete portrait to design against.

---

## Young Teens (14–16) — The Archetype

**Jakub, 16.** Jakub has a Saturday job — about £60–70 in his account by Sunday, gone by Wednesday. Nando's with his mates, a FIFA pack, the trainers he'd been eyeing. He's not irresponsible. He just has no framework. Money arrives, money leaves, nothing bad has happened yet.

He's got a 43-day Duolingo streak he's mildly obsessed with. He doesn't care about French. He cares about the streak.

**Money just got real — but he hasn't noticed.** At 13, money was coins from grandparents. At 16, it lands in his account every weekend. Interest, credit, compounding — those are adult problems for a later version of himself. The product can't lead with that version. It has to meet the one that exists right now.

---

### Learning Style

**Decisions before concepts.** Jakub doesn't engage with financial principles in the abstract — but he'll lean into figuring out a scenario. A scenario with someone his age, a real amount, a genuine choice with stakes he recognises. He won't notice he's learning. That's the design target.

**Rewards have to be visible and immediate.** A completed level, a streak held, a new unlock — these are not motivational extras. This is the product, not the stuff he's learning. Without something accumulating, there's nothing to protect and nothing to come back for.

**Short, fast, forgiving.** The moment it feels like a test, he's gone. Under three minutes per session is the design constraint.

**Tone calibration.** Credible enough not to feel talked down to, warm enough not to feel like revision.

---

### Relationship with Money

**Incurious confidence.** Jakub doesn't worry about money. He has some, he spends it, it comes back. The future is not now — and anything learning content that opens with adult outcomes loses him immediately.

**Status without stress.** What he does respond to is the quiet discomfort of not being in on something. When his older brother talks about his salary, he goes silent. He doesn't want to be the one who doesn't get it — he just hasn't found a way in that feels worth the effort.

**Thin and adult-scaled.** He knows what his parents have told him and what school has covered. Neither speaks to his actual situation: what to do with £60 that arrived Sunday and is gone by Wednesday. Content, characters, and scenarios must live inside his frame — his earnings, his spending, his goals.

**Curiosity arrives in flashes.** A charge he didn't expect. A conversation he half-overheard. It runs out quickly because nothing meets it at the right level.

---

### Money Monsters' Appeal

**Money is becoming real.** Real enough for the content to land. The game logic keeps it motivating.

**The penny-drop is decision making.** The emotion to deliver is the small, satisfying click of something adult and opaque suddenly making sense. The evaluation of different choices to be made and the post-answer explanation — *why* he got it right or wrong — is where the app earns his respect. 

---

## Older Teens (17–19) — The Archetype

**Theo, 17.** Theo works weekends in a café — roughly £250 a month whilst at college. He saves some of it: a Monzo pot building toward driving lessons, the rest on going out and food. He thinks of himself as pretty sensible with money. Mostly he is.

He asks ChatGPT for any kind of answer and gets served a stream of content on Tiktok from people his age he respects; he gets served some 'Finfluencers' who talk about getting rich at his age.

---

### Learning Style

**Relevance is the gate.** Theo won't engage with content that doesn't connect to a situation he's in or can clearly see approaching — credit cards, first payslips, scam avoidance, what student loans actually mean. The closer to now, the more attention he brings.

**Peer-level, not expert.** The tone that works is someone who's figured this out and is passing it on — not a professional from above, not a children's app from below. Scenario-based dialogue hits this register; static explainer text doesn't.

**Topic navigation, not linear lock.** Theo already knows some things. Being forced through basics to reach what he actually wants is a fast path out.

**Gamification must have stakes.** Collection without consequence reads as cosmetic. The mechanic needs to build toward something real — unlocks that change the experience, rewards that feel earned.

---

### Relationship with Money

**Low-grade confidence, unexamined gaps.** Theo rates himself capable with money. That confidence is mostly warranted — but it masks gaps he hasn't yet been asked about. The right moment to surface them is onboarding: not as failure, but as curiosity. 

**The horizon is visible.** University or work, driving, maybe a flat share — the transition is within two years. Content addressing what's coming lands as useful, not hypothetical.

**No trusted source exists.** YouTube assumes salaries he doesn't have. TikTok gives fragments. School PSHE covered bank accounts once in a lesson he half-remembers. 

---

### Money Monsters' Appeal

**Content carries the weight.** Theo will stay if the app keeps delivering the feeling of getting sharper. He'll tolerate a game he finds a bit young if the substance is real. The gameplay alone will never bring him back.

**The proposition names something true.** Nobody explained this. The onboarding baseline check should surface his gaps — not as failure, but as appetite. Done right, that moment creates the motivation to continue.
