## Key Strategic Choices to be Made

There are some strategic choices to be made, presented below in order which they should be considered as each decision will impact the others.

---

### 1. Segment Targeting

The app spans 13–25 — three audiences with incompatible expectations. Designing for all dilutes everything.

- **v1 bullseye: 14–16 year olds** — money is becoming increasingly real, app aesthetic lands, gamification resonates
- **v2 investment: 16–19 year olds** — investment in age-specific content require content works; further gamification and utility required to be credible
- **Deprioritise: 12–13s and Young Adults** — 12-13 years money is too abstract; YAs requires significant product adaptation

*For detail see [[12. Segment Verdicts]]*

### 2. Learning Experience

Three design decisions sit inside this choice — each shaping the others.

#### Content Modalities

The app defaults to multiple-choice and static text. This invites pattern-matching rather than reasoning, and the absence of multimedia (video, audio, animation) limits engagement for an audience raised on TikTok and YouTube.

- **Static-only format is a ceiling on engagement** — particularly for Young Teens, where attention span is short and media expectations are high
- **Varied input types** — scenario walkthroughs, drag-and-drop, simulated account interactions — better reflect real financial decisions and sustain attention longer
- **A feasibility decision is needed now** — which modalities and media formats are possible for v1, so the question mix is designed around that answer rather than defaulting by omission

*For detail see [[10. Lesson Experience - Learning Mechanics and Comprehension]]*

#### Topic Selection

The same topic lands differently depending on who's learning it and what's live in their financial life.

- **Segment-specific topic maps are required** — saving means something different at 14 than at 17; credit cards are preparation for YTs, live decisions for YAs
- **Without a deliberate curriculum by segment**, content risks being too simple for some and too abstract for others simultaneously

*For detail see [[4. Topics and Content - What to teach; based on research exploration and JTBD assessment]]*

#### Learning Loop

The current loop runs **Lesson → Monster** — a reward loop, not a learning loop. The app has the structural foundations: lesson content works, the progression map motivates, monsters give collection something to collect. What's missing is the stage between Learn and Progress: **Practice**.

Decision simulation — scenario-based challenges where users make a choice and see the consequence — is arguably the app's most powerful lever. It's what separates a financial literacy product from a financial quiz: not testing what you know, but rehearsing what you'd actually do. The "help this person decide" format is already the app's strongest moment in research. It needs to be sequenced in as a required stage after every lesson, not left as an occasional feature.

The full loop the product needs: **Learn → Practice → Progress → Return.** [[7. Topics and Content|Section 7]] has mapped practice mechanics per topic. The scoping decision for v1 is which topics get the full loop first — and committing to Practice as a non-optional stage in the user flow.

*For detail see [[13. The Learning Loop]]*

### 3. Distribution Model

The currentfinancial literacy market splits into **pull** (users seek it out) and **push** (institutions place it in front of them). No "Duolingo for finance" app has yet to prove consumer pull at scale — because unlike language learning, young people have no compelling external trigger to keep coming back. Money Monsters currently relies on pull it has not yet earned.

- **Pull apps succeed because real money creates recurrence** — teen banking apps (GoHenry, Greenlight) work because users are managing actual money; education is the wrapper, not the reason. Money Monsters has the education without the utility anchor.
- **Push is how scale happens in this category** — NatWest MoneySense reaches 2.2m young people via schools; Doshi reaches 200k+ via bank partnerships; Barclays LifeSkills via curriculum mandate.
- **The constraint is not product quality — it is demand.** The choice is whether to keep betting on consumer pull or to pursue institutional distribution that removes the discovery problem entirely.
- **This choice has near-term build implications.** Committing to a push route early means scoping white-label/embed capability, designing co-branding into the UX, aligning content to PSHE curriculum or bank account-opening triggers, and building a B2B pitch — none of which can be cleanly retrofitted after v1 ships.

## What's Working

**The Duolingo mental model transfers.** A large number (\~75%) of participants reached for Duolingo as their reference point, unprompted and positively. The level-map layout, locked/unlocked progression, and streak concept carry associations of accessible, habitual, low-stakes learning — a strong foundation for financial education.

**Scenario-based lessons are the right creative vehicle.** The dialogue format feels like a conversation, not a download. The "help this person" framing is lower-stakes than direct testing — it invites reasoning rather than self-judgment. Participants across all segments engaged more deeply with scenario questions than conceptual ones.

**The proposition "Money Stuff Nobody Explains" connects — particularly** This framing positions financial knowledge as insider access — things you should have been told, not things you missed. It situates the knowledge gap in the system, not the user. For Young Teens it activates curiosity; for gap-aware Young Adults it earns credibility.

**Content topics are real and relevant.** For Young Teens: social spending, impulse buying, goal-directed saving. For Older Teens: scam avoidance, first earnings, the 50/30/20 rule. For Young Adults: compound interest, credit card traps, savings products. The research surfaced specific topic priorities by segment that map directly to their actual Jobs to Be Done.

**The underlying app design works.** The progression map with locked and unlocked states, the bite-sized lesson chunks, the try-again mechanic, the post-answer explanations — these work across all segments. The problem is not how the app functions; it's the visual style and the gamification layer that hasn't yet been built on top.

## The Core Insight: The Confidence Trap — Why Users Won't Learn What They Think They Already Know

Two compounding patterns surfaced across both markets:

- **The Confidence–Competence Gap (Dunning-Kruger):** Young people rate themselves financially confident, but that confidence almost always reflects one skill — saving. Understanding of actual mechanisms falls well short. Users who think they "get it" won't seek education — the onboarding must *reveal* this gap to create motivation.
- **The Intention–Action Gap:** Even genuine understanding doesn't automatically drive behaviour in context — like knowing nutrition but reaching for the crisps. And what to do with money is subjective and contextual, not right or wrong. The app builds *informed* trade-offs, not moral instruction.

**What this means for the product:** Onboarding must *calibrate*, not just assess — surface the confidence gap to create curiosity ("huh, I thought I knew that"). Content must teach how money actually works (mechanisms, not morals). And decision simulation — presenting a real financial choice, letting users commit, then showing the consequence — closes the intention–action gap in a way a quiz never can. It's the app's most differentiated capability and the one most worth building the loop around.
