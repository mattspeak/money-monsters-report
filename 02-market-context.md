# 2. Market Context

## The constraint for Money Monsters is not product quality; it is demand

Layering the participant findings with the previous [[Expert Review]], user uptake in financial literacy solutions largely is stimulated by 'pull' rather than 'push' sources:

| Pull                                                                                                                                                                                                            | Push                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Utility** — teen banking (GoHenry, Greenlight, Gimi) and investing apps (Bloom, Stockpile). <br>Managing real money creates functional recurrence; education is the wrapper. Greenlight: 6.5M users           | **Schools & youth programmes** — MoneyTime, Young Enterprise, Money Ready. Captive audiences via curriculum. Natwest's programme: reaching 2.2m young people                           |
| **Situational** — TikTok/YouTube, AI. Answers an immediate question; massive reach, no depth or habit building. Gen Z is almost 5× more likely to use social media for financial advice than older generations. | **Institutional Partnerships** — free resources/app. E,g. Doshi (closest to Money Monsters, 18-25), NatWest MoneySense, Barclays LifeSkills. Broad distribution and regulatory mandate |

Money Monsters sits outside both pull categories. 

It aspires to generate pull through gamification and habit — but where Duolingo succeeds because users have a concrete external reason to learn a language (travel, exams, relationships), financial literacy for a 13-year-old has no equivalent trigger. No "Duolingo for finance" app has proven this model at scale. 
## The Distribution Implication

Growth through consumer pull alone is a bet on a model this category has not yet proven. The realistic path to scale is institutional — placing the product in front of audiences who don't need to find it themselves:

- **Schools:** Financial literacy sits within PSHE and Citizenship in UK secondary schools; in the Czech Republic it has been mandatory in the national curriculum since 2013. A curriculum-aligned, easy-setup version opens bulk routes in both markets.
- **Banks:** NatWest and Barclays have the school relationships, the CSR mandate, and the budget — but not a compelling product. Money Monsters could operate as the delivery vehicle behind an existing bank programme.
- **Educational platforms:** Twinkl, Oak National Academy, and Kahoot have deep teacher penetration and active demand for classroom-ready content. Distribution without cold outreach.
