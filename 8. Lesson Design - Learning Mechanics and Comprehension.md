# 8. Lesson Design: Learning Mechanics and Comprehension

- TOC
{:toc}

## 8.1 The Creative Vehicle: Dialogue vs. Lecture

The research stimulus used a dialogue-based lesson format: financial scenarios played out through character conversations, with the learner prompted to help a character make a decision, choose between options, or identify the right approach. 

### 8.1.1 Why the dialogue/scenario format is resonating

The scenario format works because it inverts the typical relationship between information and decision. This creates two advantages that were consistently visible across participants:

1. **Reducing perceived difficulty.** The scenario format makes financial content feel navigable — participants reason from experience rather than memorised definitions. Levi (17) "always found a relatable answer among the options."

2. **Creating emotional engagement.** The dialogue format feels like a conversation, not a download (Enoch, 18). The "help this person" framing is lower-stakes than direct testing — it invites reasoning rather than self-judgment.

The principle: the dialogue/scenario format should be the primary creative vehicle for Money Monsters lessons. Presenting information in the context of a decision is the effective mechanism by which the app creates the conditions for the **knowing–doing gap** to close. 

### 8.1.2 The role of character-based scenarios

Two design principles emerged from the research.

**Character consistency:** characters must have stable, clearly established identities that persist throughout a session. Aiden (15) described confusion about whether he was playing *as* the monster character or *collecting* it — a character identity problem that broke the scenario logic. The learner should always know whether they are inhabiting a character or advising one.

**Character relevance:** characters' age, income level, and life situation should approximate those of the target user. Sam (14) was drawn to Mr. Money because a human character "who has done this as well" carries more credibility. For older segments, scenarios need to reflect real financial complexity — rent decisions, credit card choices, actual employment income — otherwise the learning feels abstract rather than rehearsal for real life.

## 8.2 Question Design

### 8.2.1 How questions are presented: what works

The most effective question formats combined **scenario framing, appropriate contextual detail, and an outcome orientation** (what should this person do?) rather than a knowledge orientation (what is the correct definition of X?). 

**Specificity matters.** The key detail a learner needs to answer a question must be present on the same screen as the question — not the screen before it. Questions that embed the relevant information in the scenario itself consistently performed better than those requiring participants to recall from an earlier screen.

**Answer option quality matters as much as the question.** Effective multiple choice requires all options to be plausible. When wrong answers are too easily dismissed, the question has no learning value — the participant is pattern-matching rather than reasoning.

**Input modality should not default to multiple choice.** Even well-designed MCQ presented in an engaging format (e.g. logging into a simulated bank account) benefits from being mixed with other response types. For v1, we strongly recommend working with the development team to establish which input modalities are technically feasible — and designing the question mix to use them. Varied modalities sustain attention and better reflect how financial decisions are actually made.

### 8.2.2 Try-again and feedback mechanics

The retry mechanic — requiring re-engagement rather than simply moving past a wrong answer — was consistently appreciated. Enoch (18) valued that "the app doesn't move on until the question is answered correctly." In financial education, the goal is understanding, not a passing score; moving on without grasping why produces a false sense of completion.

Post-answer explanation adds further value whether the answer was right or wrong (Mia, 25). The principle: every question should end with understanding, not just a result. Keep explanations brief, plain-language, and conversational.

### 8.2.3 UX principles for learning: keeping reference information accessible

**Principle 1:** Participants responded well to access to deeper definitions and worked examples at the point of learning. Tapping the *"What is compound interest?"* button in the answer feedback overlay — which broke down the concept further — was a well-received mechanism. Additional explanatory depth, where it deepens understanding rather than adds friction, is an asset rather than a burden.

**Principle 2:** Where content spans multiple screens, the critical decision-relevant detail must be on the screen where the decision is made. As Mohammad noted: *"where content is split over a couple of screens, it's really important that the key detail is on the second screen."*

## 8.3 Lesson Length and Session Duration

### 8.3.1 Current length: broadly appropriate for YT/OT; format mismatch for YA

The research stimulus was designed to give respondents a flavour of the learning material, not to precisely test lesson duration — so findings on length should be treated with some caution. 

The broad picture: the short-form format worked for Young Teens and Older Teens, where it matched attention span and usage habit. This group also, envisaged longer app usage if there was greater elements of gamification (see gamification section).

For Young Adults, there was clearer frustration — lessons felt too brief to adequately explore a concept. Mohammad (23) wanted longer, 5-minute sessions; Zain (20) described the content as "scraping the top of things." Althea (19) and Grace (19) described a situational rather than daily usage pattern — reaching for the app when a specific financial question arose.

### 8.3.2 The mobile context: commuting, idle time, short-burst use

Across all segments, participants described usage as fundamentally mobile and opportunistic. Enoch (18) described the lesson length as "perfect for bus journeys." Sam (14) envisioned checking the app in the morning; Ovie (15) connected daily engagement to notification-driven habit formation. The usage context shapes the conditions under which learning must occur — design must serve those actual conditions: i.e. short/bitesized framing.
