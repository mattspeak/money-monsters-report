# 12. Lesson Experience: Learning Mechanics and Comprehension

## The Dialogue/Scenario Format

### What works
- Scenario-based learning reduces perceived difficulty — users reason from experience rather than memorised definitions
- The "help this person" framing is lower-stakes than direct testing — it invites reasoning rather than self-judgment
- Dialogue feels like a conversation, not a download — categorically different from text-heavy competitors

> *"It's making you think about real life situations and how you would react... It's better than some apps who don't use real life."* (Olivia, 14, UK)

> *"I like how it has storylines to everything. Rather than just questions."* (Lottie, 13, UK)

### Character design requirements
- **Identity clarity:** characters must have stable, established identities — confusion about whether you play *as* or *alongside* a character breaks scenario logic
- **Contextual relevance:** character age, income, and life situation should match the target segment — older users disengage when scenarios feel abstract or young

> *"I think the choice of Mr. Money is very good. Because it makes it seem real... It makes it feel like that person who is helping him has done this as well."* (Sam, 14, UK)

**Recommendation:** Make dialogue/scenario the primary creative vehicle. It's how Money Monsters closes the knowing–doing gap — users learn through decisions, not explanations.

## Question Design

### What works
- Questions that embed the relevant information in the scenario itself perform better than those requiring recall from a previous screen
- All answer options must be plausible — easily-dismissed wrong answers have no learning value; users pattern-match rather than reason
- The retry mechanic (re-engagement required after a wrong answer) was consistently valued — moving on without understanding produces false completion
- Post-answer explanation adds value whether right or wrong — keep it brief, plain-language, and conversational

> *"The app doesn't move on until the question is answered correctly."* (Enoch, 18, UK)

> *"I like how it gives you facts once you do a question rather than just saying yes."* (Lottie, 13, UK)

### Input modality and reference access
- Multiple choice should not be the default — even well-designed MCQ benefits from being mixed with other response types
- "Tap for more detail" (e.g. inline glossary on the answer screen) was a well-received mechanism for users who want to go deeper
- Critical decision-relevant detail must be on the same screen as the decision — not the screen before it

> *"It was very just clicking a bunch of options. If it had different stuff... if it had different tasks."* (Zain, 13, UK)

> *"It was hard to remember the detail in the question because I had to go back to the previous screen."* (Mohammad, 23, UK)

**Recommendation:** In v1, establish which input modalities are technically feasible, then design the question mix to use them. Varied modalities sustain attention and better reflect how financial decisions are actually made.

## Content Format: Static vs. Video-Native

- Current format (text, illustrations, MCQ) is functional but in tension with how young people actually encounter financial content
- Short-form video is the default modality for financial content among Older Teens — TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube are primary channels
- Video's advantage is relatability (real people, real situations) and visceral impact — not just format
- The dialogue/scenario format partially compensates for absent rich media by creating narrative momentum — but this is a ceiling, not a solution

> *"I mainly learn about money through TikTok and Instagram — seeing real people talk about it makes it feel more relevant."* (Levi, 17, UK)

> *"I learned about scams through YouTube. Watching what happens makes it feel real — like it could actually happen to you."* (Aiden, 15, UK)

{additional quote from CZ}
### Where rich media earns its place
- **Concept introductions:** short animated or voice-narrated explainers at the start of new topics — animated visuals of how numbers grow are categorically more intuitive than a paragraph
- **Scenario detailing:** audio/ambient/animation additions strengthen immersion — especially for Older Teens where scenario plausibility directly affects engagement
- **Post-answer "go deeper":** optional TikTok-style explainers (15–30 sec) after completing a question — extends dwell time for curious users without burdening the core flow

**Recommendation:** Roadmap selective introduction of video and audio at concept introductions and scenario moments. This is where rich media earns its place through comprehension and emotional engagement — not novelty.

## Lesson Length and Session Design

### What we found
- Bitesize format is correct for all segments
- Session tolerance (from [[4. Money Attitudes, Behaviors and Habits]] dip data): Young Teens ~5–8 min; Older Teens ~10–12 min
- Usage is fundamentally mobile and opportunistic — bus journeys, morning check-ins, idle moments
- For Older Teens, the time ceiling is *earned* — abstract or poorly-contextualised content triggers early exit regardless of intended lesson length

### Implications for lesson architecture
- **Young Teens:** individual lessons completable in 3–5 min; reward mechanics — not more content — are the mechanism for extending sessions
- **Older Teens:** lessons can run slightly longer, but only where content stays tightly anchored to the scenario
- Design must serve actual usage conditions — short-burst, mobile, low-commitment contexts

**Recommendation:** Design sessions to extend through loops and incentives, not lesson length — especially for Young Teens. For all segments, bitesize is the right architecture.
